## A universal language for coffee

Imagine a producer in Ethiopia needs to tell a buyer in Tokyo exactly how their coffee tastes. They can't mail a cup. They need a standardized system both know and that produces consistent results regardless of who uses it or where they are. That system is the SCA (Specialty Coffee Association) tasting protocol.

Tasting (or "cupping" in English) isn't simply "trying coffee." It's a scientific method with controlled parameters that lets you evaluate coffee objectively and reproducibly. It's the tool used by producers, buyers, roasters, and baristas across the entire supply chain to speak the same language.

You don't need to be professional to use it. Learning the tasting protocol gives you a framework to evaluate any coffee you try, and over time, develop precise vocabulary to describe what you perceive.

## The setup: why every detail matters

The SCA protocol is exacting about parameters because any variation changes the result. If you compare coffees brewed differently, you're not comparing the coffees — you're comparing the preparations.

**Ratio:** 8.25g of coffee per 150ml of water. This ratio is calibrated to produce extraction that reveals the coffee's character without overloading it.

**Grind:** Slightly coarser than for drip filtration. The standard says 70-75% of particles should pass through a #20 sieve (850 microns). In practice, think of coarse sand.

**Water:** 93°C (200°F), quality per SCA standards (TDS 75-250 ppm, neutral pH). Water is poured directly on ground coffee in the bowl.

**Bowls:** Ceramic or tempered glass bowls of 150-260ml, all identical. Minimum two bowls per sample are prepared to detect inconsistencies between bowls (which may indicate defects in some beans).

**Spoons:** Tasting spoons (similar to soup spoons but more rounded and deep). Rinsed between samples in clean hot water.

**Time:** 4 minutes of infusion before breaking the crust. Total evaluation: the entire session may last 15-20 minutes per table as coffees cool.

## The phases of tasting

### 1. Fragrance (dry coffee)

Before adding water, smell the freshly ground coffee in the bowl. This is fragrance — volatile compounds released by grinding. Bring your nose to the bowl, briefly cover it with your hand to concentrate aromas, and breathe.

On the SCA form, you evaluate the intensity and quality of fragrance. Is it intense or weak? Is it pleasant or are there negative notes? Fresh, quality coffees have intense fragrance; old coffee smells like little.

### 2. Aroma (coffee with water)

Pour 93°C water over ground coffee, filling the bowl. A crust forms (a layer of ground coffee floating on the surface). Wait 3-4 minutes. During this time, trapped gases under the crust release.

At 3-4 minutes, you break the crust: with the back of your spoon, gently push the crust toward the back of the bowl with 3 movements. While breaking the crust, bring your nose close — this is the moment of maximum aroma release. It's the first deep impression of the coffee.

Evaluate aroma the same way: intensity and quality.

### 3. Surface cleanup

After breaking the crust, with two spoons remove the foam and particles floating on top. Don't agitate — just remove what's on the surface. The goal is clean liquid for evaluation.

### 4. Slurp

This is where mouth evaluation starts. With the spoon, take a small amount of coffee and slurp it strongly — the sound should be audible. It's not a polite sip; it's a deliberate spray that disperses coffee across your entire tongue and sends volatiles to the retro-nasal receptors.

The slurping technique is fundamental: if you simply drink the coffee, you perceive much less. The slurp aerosolizes it, creating thousands of micro-droplets that cover more of your mouth surface and release more aromatic compounds to your nose.

You evaluate at multiple temperatures. You start when coffee is still hot (~70°C) and continue as it cools. Each temperature reveals different aspects.

## The SCA tasting form

The form evaluates coffee in these categories, each scored from 6 to 10 (in 0.25 increments):

### Fragrance/Aroma
Described above. You evaluate the quality of dry fragrance and wet aroma. Combined score.

### Flavor
The central category. Evaluates the complexity, clarity, and quality of flavors perceived in your mouth. Are the flavors defined or confused? Are they pleasant or unpleasant? Is there complexity (multiple notes) or is it one-dimensional?

### Aftertaste
What remains after swallowing or spitting. Does the flavor linger pleasantly? Does it transform into something sweet and clean, or become bitter and astringent? Great coffee has a long, sweet, pleasant aftertaste. Mediocre coffee has a short or unpleasant aftertaste.

### Acidity
You don't evaluate if there's much or little acidity, but the quality of that acidity. Is it bright, clean, fruity? That's good. Is it sour, vinegary, sharp? That's bad. Intensity is also marked on a separate scale (low to high), but intensity doesn't determine the score — quality does.

### Body
The tactile sensation in your mouth. Is it silky, creamy, velvety? Good. Is it watery, gritty, astringent? Bad. Again, you don't evaluate if the body is heavy or light (that's intensity), but the quality of that sensation.

### Uniformity
Do different bowls of the same sample taste the same? If one bowl tastes different, it indicates inconsistency in the lot — possibly defective beans mixed in. 2 points are awarded per uniform bowl (maximum 10 for 5 bowls).

### Clean Cup
Are there any strange, cloudy, or contaminating flavors? A clean cup has no flavors that shouldn't be there. 2 points awarded per clean bowl.

### Sweetness
Does the coffee have pleasant natural sweetness? 2 points per bowl showing sweetness. Absence of sweetness indicates green beans, incomplete fermentation, or other defects.

### Overall
Your holistic impression of the coffee. Do you like it? Would you have it again? This is the most subjective category and is designed to capture the total experience the individual categories don't fully cover.

### Defects
Defects are subtracted from the total. They're classified as "taint" (detectable but not dominant, multiplied x2) or "fault" (dominant, multiplied x4). Common defects: fermented, phenolic, moldy, earthy, rubbery, potato (the "potato defect" typical of Burundi/Rwanda).

## The final score

All categories are summed and defects subtracted. The result is a number out of 100:

**90-100:** Exceptional. Rare competition coffees.
**85-89.99:** Excellent. High-end specialty coffee.
**80-84.99:** Very good. Minimum threshold for "specialty coffee."
**<80:** Not specialty coffee per SCA.

Most coffees you buy in a specialty shop are between 82 and 88. Those exceeding 90 are scarce and usually sold at auction for very high prices.

## Home tasting: how to adapt the protocol

You don't need 5 bowls or follow the protocol exactly. Here's a simplified but effective version:

**What you need:** 2-3 identical bowls (ceramic cups work), soup spoon, kettle, scale, 2-3 different coffees to compare.

**Setup:** 12g of coffee per bowl, ground coarse (like for French press). Pour 200ml of 93°C water directly on the coffee. One bowl per coffee is enough to start.

**Simplified process:**

Smell the dry coffee (fragrance). Pour water. Wait 4 minutes. Break the crust bringing your nose close. Clean the surface. Wait 2-3 more minutes for it to cool. Start slurping.

Evaluate each coffee on these 5 simplified categories: aroma, acidity, sweetness, body, and aftertaste. Don't worry about numerical scores at first — use a simple scale: poor, fair, good, very good, excellent.

The most important thing is to compare. Tasting one coffee alone doesn't tell you much. Tasting two coffees side by side makes differences obvious. A washed Ethiopian next to a natural Brazilian shows you the difference between bright acidity and chocolatey sweetness immediately.

## Common tasting mistakes

**Evaluating with the coffee too hot.** Novice tasters start slurping right after breaking the crust. At >70°C you lose many nuances. Wait for it to drop to 60°C or less before serious evaluation.

**Not slurping with enough force.** The slurp is loud on purpose. If you're not making noise, you're not aerosolizing the coffee enough and perceiving less.

**Looking for specific flavors instead of describing what you perceive.** Don't try to "find the cherry" the bag mentions. Describe what you feel, in your own words. Vocabulary comes later.

**Not cleaning the spoon between samples.** It contaminates the next coffee and makes comparisons worthless.

**Tasting alone without calibration.** If you only taste alone, your scores mean nothing to anyone else. Tasting gains value when you do it with others and compare perceptions. Find tasting groups in your city or online.

## Practical exercise

Organize a home tasting with 2-3 coffees:

1. Prepare the bowls (12g coffee, 200ml water at 93°C per bowl)
2. Evaluate fragrance of each one (dry, before adding water)
3. Pour water, wait 4 minutes, break crusts
4. Clean surfaces, wait for them to cool to ~60°C
5. Slurp each coffee multiple times, alternating between bowls
6. For each coffee, note: aroma (what you smell), acidity (bright/flat), sweetness (present/absent), body (light/medium/full), aftertaste (pleasant/unpleasant), and overall impression

Don't worry if at first you only perceive "coffee." With practice, you'll start distinguishing clear differences.

## Key concepts from this lesson

- SCA tasting is a standardized protocol for evaluating coffee objectively and reproducibly
- Fixed parameters (8.25g/150ml ratio, specific grind, 93°C) eliminate external variables
- You evaluate: fragrance/aroma, flavor, aftertaste, acidity, body, uniformity, cleanliness, sweetness, and overall score
- Strong slurping is technique, not rudeness: it aerosolizes coffee for better perception
- 80+ points = specialty coffee; 85+ = excellent; 90+ = exceptional
- Comparing coffees side by side is much more informative than tasting them alone
- You can adapt the protocol at home with basic equipment and 2-3 coffees
