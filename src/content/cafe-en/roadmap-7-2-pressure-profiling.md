## Beyond 9 bars

In Phase 2 you learned that espresso extracts at ~9 bars of pressure. That's true as a standard, but it's a simplification. In reality, pressure during an espresso shot is not constant — and manipulating it intentionally is one of the most powerful tools an advanced barista has.

Scott Rao was one of the first to document and popularize pressure profiling in the context of specialty coffee. His approach challenges the idea that an espresso shot should have constant pressure from beginning to end. Instead, he proposes that different extraction phases benefit from different pressure levels.

## Pre-infusion: the first contact

Pre-infusion is the period at the beginning of extraction where water contacts the coffee at low pressure before the pump reaches maximum pressure. Most machines do some type of pre-infusion, but duration and shape vary enormously.

### Short pre-infusion (standard)

Most commercial machines have short pre-infusion (2-5 seconds). The pump activates, pressure rises quickly, and within seconds it reaches 9 bars. This basic pre-infusion simply fills the chamber and saturates the coffee before full pressure.

### Long pre-infusion (Rao methodology)

Rao recommends significantly longer pre-infusions: 8-15 seconds at low pressure (2-4 bars) before ramping to extraction pressure. Why?

**Uniform saturation.** When water reaches the coffee bed at low pressure, it has time to saturate all particles uniformly. Without pre-infusion, water at 9 bars immediately seeks paths of least resistance before the coffee is completely saturated — this causes channeling.

**Fines migration.** During pre-infusion, fine particles migrate and redistribute within the coffee bed, creating a natural filtration layer. This stabilizes flow for subsequent extraction.

**Channeling reduction.** The most significant benefit: long pre-infusion drastically reduces channeling because the coffee bed settles and compacts naturally before full pressure arrives. It's like letting soil settle in water before forcing water through it.

### How to implement long pre-infusion

**On machines with pre-infusion control:** Some machines (La Marzocco Strada, Decent DE1, Slayer, Synesso) allow you to program pre-infusion duration and pressure directly.

**On E61 machines (manual lever):** The E61 group has natural "mechanical" pre-infusion: when you lift the lever to the first position, water enters by gravity (~2 bars). Hold in this position for 5-10 seconds before moving to full position.

**"Blooming espresso" (pre-infusion with pause):** A variant where you open the water for 3-5 seconds at low pressure, then close completely and wait 20-30 seconds (the coffee "blooms" like in pour-over), then start extraction at full pressure. This technique produces shots with exceptional sweetness but requires experimentation.

## Pressure profiles according to Rao

### Declining pressure profile

The profile Rao recommends most frequently. Pressure starts high and gradually declines during extraction:

**0-10 sec:** Pre-infusion at 2-4 bars. Uniform bed saturation.
**10-15 sec:** Gentle pressure ramp to 8-9 bars. Extraction begins.
**15-30 sec:** Gradual decline from 9 to 6 bars. As solids extract and the bed becomes more porous.

**Why decline?** As extraction proceeds, the coffee becomes more porous — it's lost many solubles. If you maintain 9 bars of pressure on an increasingly permeable bed, flow accelerates at the end of the shot. That acceleration extracts bitter and astringent compounds that were protected inside particles. Reducing pressure compensates for lost resistance and maintains constant flow.

The result: sweeter shots, less bitterness at the finish, and greater flavor clarity.

### Flat profile

The classic profile: constant 9 bars from beginning to end (after pre-infusion). This is what most standard commercial machines do.

**Advantages:** Simple, reproducible, requires no special equipment.
**Disadvantages:** Flow accelerates at the end as coffee depletes, causing over-extraction of the final compounds.

### Rising pressure profile

Starts at low pressure and gradually increases. Less common but interesting for certain coffees.

**Use:** Very dense, light-roasted coffees that need lots of energy to extract completely. The initial low pressure reduces early channeling risk, and rising pressure keeps extraction active as more difficult solubles are released.

## Extended ratios: 1:3 and beyond

One of Rao's most influential contributions to modern espresso was demonstrating that longer extraction ratios can produce delicious espresso. Traditional Italian ratios (1:1 to 1:2) produce concentrated shots but often under-extracted.

### Rao's argument

With a 1:2 ratio (18g coffee → 36g espresso), typical extraction is 18-20%. Rao showed that pushing extraction to 22-23% — using 1:2.5 to 1:3 ratios — can produce shots with more sweetness, less bitterness, and more complexity. Yes, they're less concentrated, but flavor quality compensates.

**1:2 (standard):** 18g in → 36g out. Intense, concentrated. Extraction ~18-20%.
**1:2.5:** 18g → 45g. Sweeter, more balanced. Extraction ~20-22%.
**1:3:** 18g → 54g. Maximum sweetness, lighter body. Extraction ~22-24%.

### When to use longer ratios

Light-roasted, high-density coffees particularly benefit from longer ratios. These coffees have more available solubles than dark roasts but need more water to extract them. A 1:3 with a light-roasted Ethiopian can reveal fruity and floral notes that 1:2 never reaches.

Medium to dark roasted coffees generally work better with 1:2 to 1:2.5. The additional extraction of a 1:3 can pull out carbonized and bitter compounds the roast already created.

### The limit

Beyond 1:3.5, most coffees start to taste "watery" — concentration drops so much the flavor becomes diluted. But the sweet spot of 1:2.5 to 1:3 is territory many baristas still haven't explored, and Rao argues they're missing some of the best possible shots.

## Turbo shots: speed as a tool

A recent development influenced by Rao's philosophy is the "turbo shot": significantly coarser grind than normal espresso, long ratio (1:3 to 1:4), and short extraction time (15-20 seconds). High pressure moves lots of water quickly through a relatively coarse grind.

**The result:** An espresso that extracts to 22-24% in half the time. High extraction produces sweetness and complexity, while short time prevents over-extraction of bitter compounds that appear with prolonged contact.

Turbo shots aren't for all coffees or all palates, but they're an example of how Rao's mentality (questioning established parameters) has opened new possibilities in espresso.

## Practical implementation

### If your machine has no pressure control

Most home machines and many commercial machines don't allow you to adjust pressure during the shot. In that case:

**Focus on pre-infusion.** If your machine has manual pre-infusion mode (like the E61 group), use it. Even 5-8 seconds of pre-infusion at low pressure significantly improve the shot.

**Adjust flow with grind.** Without pressure control, your main tool is grind. A finer grind increases bed resistance and reduces flow — it's an indirect way to control the "effective pressure" the coffee experiences.

**Experiment with longer ratios.** You don't need pressure profiling to try 1:2.5 or 1:3 ratios. Just let the shot run longer. Try and compare.

### If your machine has pressure control

Start with Rao's declining profile as your base:

1. Pre-infusion: 10 seconds at 3 bars
2. Gentle ramp to 9 bars over 5 seconds
3. Gradual decline to 6 bars over the next 15-20 seconds
4. Total time: ~35-40 seconds

Adjust by coffee: light roasts may benefit from more pressure (start at 9, drop to 7). Dark roasts may benefit from less overall pressure (start at 8, drop to 5).

## Practical exercise

**Ratio comparison:** Using the same coffee and grind, prepare three shots:

1. **1:2 ratio** — 18g in, stop at 36g. Note time.
2. **1:2.5 ratio** — 18g in, stop at 45g. Adjust grind if time deviates too much.
3. **1:3 ratio** — 18g in, stop at 54g. Adjust grind.

Taste all three. The 1:2 will be the most intense. The 1:3 should be the sweetest and cleanest (if it's not watery or bitter, your grind is good). Find which you prefer.

**Pre-infusion comparison (if your machine allows):**

1. **Without extended pre-infusion:** Normal extraction. Observe flow with naked portafilter.
2. **With 10 seconds of pre-infusion at low pressure:** Observe how flow is more uniform and symmetric from the start.

## Key concepts from this lesson

- Pressure during a shot doesn't need to be constant; manipulating it is a powerful tool
- Long pre-infusion (8-15 sec at 2-4 bars) reduces channeling and improves uniformity
- Declining profile (9→6 bars) compensates for bed resistance loss and reduces bitterness
- Extended ratios (1:2.5 to 1:3) can produce sweeter, more complex shots than standard 1:2
- Turbo shots (coarse grind, long ratio, short time) are an example of innovation based on this philosophy
- Even without pressure control, you can experiment with manual pre-infusion and longer ratios
- The Rao mentality: question established parameters and let flavor guide your decisions
