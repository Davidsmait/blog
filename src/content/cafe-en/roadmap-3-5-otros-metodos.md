## Beyond pour-over

We've covered the big three of filtered coffee: Chemex, V60, and Aeropress. But the world of specialty coffee has more tools, each with its own extraction philosophy. In this lesson we explore four additional methods that complement your arsenal: French press (the immersion classic), Clever Dripper (the perfect hybrid), cold brew (cold extraction), and siphon (the theater of coffee).

You don't need to master all of them. But understanding how each works gives you perspective on extraction and helps you choose the right method based on the coffee, the moment, and the result you're looking for.

## French press (pressurized pot)

French press is pure immersion: ground coffee + hot water + time. No paper filter. No controlled gravity. No pressure. Just soaking.

### How it works

The coffee steeps completely in hot water for 4 minutes. Then you press the plunger with mesh screen downward to separate the coffee from the liquid. The mesh catches large particles but lets oils, micro-fines, and suspended compounds through.

### The James Hoffmann technique for French press

Hoffmann proposes a technique that contradicts decades of conventional use and produces a much cleaner cup:

**Dose:** 15g of coffee
**Water:** 250g at 96-100°C (use freshly boiled water)
**Grind:** Medium-coarse (coarser than pour-over but not as coarse as the "standard" French press you see online)
**Total time:** ~9-10 minutes (yes, much longer than you'd expect)

**0:00:** Add coffee, pour all the water. Don't stir yet.

**4:00:** You'll see a crust of coffee floating on the surface. With a spoon, gently break the crust with 2-3 movements. Most of the coffee will sink. With two spoons, remove the foam and particles floating on the surface. Discard them.

**5:00-9:00:** Wait. Touch nothing. The fines settle to the bottom by gravity. This is the secret: extra time allows particles to naturally sediment.

**9:00:** Place the plunger but DON'T press it all the way down. Just lower it to just below the liquid surface — it acts as a passive filter, not a press. Pour carefully, without tilting too much to avoid stirring the sediment at the bottom.

The result is a surprisingly clean French press, with silky body but without the mud normally associated with this method. The difference from traditional technique (4 minutes, stir, press hard) is huge.

### Strengths and limitations

**Strengths:** Dense body and rich texture (oils don't filter). Simple method with no pouring technique. Good revealer of defects — if a coffee has problems, you'll notice in French press. Economic and durable.

**Limitations:** Never as clean as a pour-over with paper filter. Sediment is always present, though Hoffmann's technique minimizes it. Not ideal for delicate light-roast coffees where clarity is key — those shine more in V60 or Chemex.

**Best for:** Medium to dark roast coffees with chocolate, nut, caramel notes. Origins like Brazil, Colombia, Sumatra.

## Clever Dripper

The Clever Dripper is an immersion dripper — it combines the best of French press (complete immersion) with the best of pour-over (paper filtration). It's probably the most underrated method in specialty coffee.

### How it works

It looks like a normal conical dripper (similar to Melitta), but it has a valve at the bottom that holds the water. The coffee soaks in complete immersion, like a French press. When you place the Clever on your cup, the valve opens and the coffee filters through paper by gravity, like a pour-over. Best of both worlds.

### Base recipe

**Dose:** 15g of coffee
**Water:** 250g at 93-96°C
**Grind:** Medium (similar to pour-over, slightly coarser is fine)
**Filter:** Melitta #4 filters or Clever-specific filters

**Setup:** Place the filter, rinse with hot water (discard the rinse water).

**0:00:** Add the coffee. Pour all the water at once. Stir 2-3 times to saturate.

**0:10:** Place the lid to maintain temperature.

**2:30-3:00:** Place the Clever on your cup or server. The valve opens automatically and coffee filters. The drawdown takes ~1-1:30 minutes.

**Total time:** ~4:00-4:30 minutes.

### Why it's so good

**Consistency.** Immersion is inherently more consistent than pour-over because it doesn't depend on your pouring technique. You pour, wait, filter. If you use the same dose, grind, and time, the result will be practically identical every time.

**Forgiving.** Unlike the V60, where a change in pouring speed changes extraction, with the Clever your only active decision is how long to let the coffee immerse. Fewer variables = fewer opportunities for error.

**Clean cup with body.** The paper filter eliminates sediment and oils like a pour-over, but complete immersion produces more body than conventional drip. It's like a clean French press.

**Ideal for evaluating new coffees.** Many professionals use the Clever to test new coffees because its consistency lets you evaluate the coffee without interference from technique.

### When to choose Clever over V60

If you're starting and frustrated with V60's inconsistency, use the Clever. If you have a new coffee and want to evaluate it without your technique interfering, use the Clever. If you want a clean, consistent coffee without effort, use the Clever. If you want maximum complexity and are willing to practice, then V60 has the advantage.

## Cold brew

Cold brew is cold extraction: ground coffee submerged in cold (or room temperature) water for 12-24 hours. It's a completely different method because it eliminates a fundamental variable: temperature.

### The science of cold extraction

Without heat, extraction is dramatically slower and more selective. Cold water extracts sweet compounds and soft notes well, but is very inefficient at extracting chlorogenic acids (responsible for bright acidity and also bitterness). The result is naturally sweet coffee, smooth, with low acidity and practically no bitterness.

This sounds ideal, but has a cost: cold brew loses complexity. The bright, fruity, floral notes that make a washed Ethiopian or Kenyan special are greatly attenuated. Cold brew levels everything down — everything tastes smooth, sweet, chocolatey.

For this reason, many specialty coffee professionals see cold brew as a method that wastes good coffee. I don't completely agree — there are coffees spectacular in cold brew (natural Brazilian, Colombian with chocolate notes). But it's true that a $50/kg competition coffee probably shines more in hot V60 than in cold brew.

### Concentrated cold brew recipe

**Dose:** 100g of coffee
**Water:** 600g at room temperature (ratio 1:6 for concentrate)
**Grind:** Coarse — like for French press or coarser. Think kosher salt.
**Time:** 16-20 hours

**Setup:** Combine coffee and water in a large jar (1-liter Mason jar works well). Stir to saturate all the coffee. Cover and place in the refrigerator or at room temperature.

**After 16-20 hours:** Filter. You can use a strainer with cloth filter, a coffee filter in a dripper, or simply a fine strainer with cheesecloth. Filter twice if you want more cleanliness.

**To serve:** The concentrate is strong. Dilute with cold water or milk in a 1:1 or 1:2 ratio depending on taste. Serve over ice.

**Duration:** The concentrate lasts 1-2 weeks refrigerated. Cold brew already diluted, 3-4 days.

### Cold brew variables

**Ratio:** 1:6 for concentrate (dilute after), 1:12-1:15 for ready-to-drink (ready without diluting).

**Temperature:** Refrigerator (~4°C) produces a softer, sweeter profile. Room temperature (~20°C) extracts a bit more and can produce slightly more complexity. The difference is subtle but exists.

**Time:** Less than 12 hours = under-extraction (watery, no sweetness). 16-20 hours = the sweet spot. More than 24 hours = starts extracting astringent and bitter compounds.

**Grind:** Coarser than any other method. If you grind fine, in 20 hours you'll have over-extraction and bitter coffee.

### Cold brew vs. iced coffee

They're not the same. Cold brew never touches hot water — it extracts cold from the start. Iced coffee (iced coffee) is prepared hot (pour-over, espresso) and cooled with ice. The flavor profile is completely different: iced coffee preserves the acidity and complexity of hot coffee, while cold brew has its characteristic smoothness.

The Japanese iced coffee technique (flash brew) pours hot coffee directly onto ice, cooling it instantly. It produces iced coffee with more character than cold brew and is faster to prepare: in 4 minutes you have cold coffee with the complexity of a pour-over.

## Siphon (vacuum brewer)

The siphon is the most visually spectacular method: it uses steam pressure to move water between two glass chambers. It looks like a 19th-century chemistry apparatus — because essentially it is (it was invented in the 1830s).

### How it works

The siphon has two chambers: a lower one with water and an upper one with coffee. When you heat the lower chamber, water rises to the upper one by steam pressure. The coffee extracts by immersion in the upper chamber. When you remove the heat source, the cooling creates a vacuum that sucks the filtered coffee back down into the lower chamber through a cloth or metal filter.

### Experience vs. practicality

Let's be direct: the siphon doesn't produce objectively better coffee than a good V60 or Clever Dripper. The cup is clean, with good body and silky texture, but there's nothing you can't achieve with simpler methods.

What the siphon offers is **theater and ritual**. It's hypnotic watching water rise, coffee extract, then be sucked back. In high-end cafes, the siphon is a customer experience tool more than a cup-quality tool. It's the method that makes people pull out their phones and take pictures.

### Basic siphon recipe

**Dose:** 20g of coffee
**Water:** 300g
**Grind:** Medium (similar to pour-over)
**Immersion time:** 1:00-1:30 minutes
**Heat source:** Butane, halogen, or alcohol burner

**Step 1:** Add water to the lower chamber. Turn on the heat source.

**Step 2:** When water starts rising to the upper chamber, add ground coffee. Stir gently to saturate.

**Step 3:** Let brew for 1:00-1:30 minutes. Stir once halfway through.

**Step 4:** Remove heat source. The vacuum sucks the filtered coffee into the lower chamber. This "drawdown" takes ~45-60 seconds.

**Step 5:** Carefully remove the upper chamber (it's hot) and pour.

### Is a siphon worth buying?

For daily use, probably not. They're fragile, require external heat, cleaning is laborious, and cloth filters need maintenance. A good siphon (Hario TCA-5, ~$80-100) is an investment justified more by the pleasure of ritual than extraction quality.

If you're drawn to the visual and ceremonial aspects of coffee, or want a method to impress guests, the siphon is unmatched. If you're after efficiency and pure quality, stick with your V60.

## Comparative summary of all filtration methods

| Method | Type | Body | Cleanliness | Difficulty | Cost | Best for |
|--------|------|--------|----------|------------|-------|------------|
| V60 | Percolation | Medium | High | High | $10 | Maximum complexity and clarity |
| Chemex | Percolation | Light | Very high | Medium | $45 | Clean, smooth, shared cups |
| Aeropress | Hybrid | Medium-high | High | Low | $35 | Experimentation, travel, versatility |
| French press | Immersion | High | Low | Low | $25 | Dense body, chocolates, nuts |
| Clever | Hybrid | Medium | High | Very low | $25 | Consistency, evaluating new coffees |
| Cold brew | Cold immersion | Medium | Medium | Low | $5* | Cold coffee, sweet, low acidity |
| Siphon | Immersion/vacuum | Medium | High | High | $80 | Theater, ritual, visual experience |

*Cold brew only needs a jar and a filter.

## What you need for this lesson

To try the methods in this lesson you only need one — don't buy them all. My recommendation by priority:

1. **Clever Dripper** (~$25) if you don't have one — it's the most consistent and underrated method
2. **French press** (~$25) if you want to explore pure immersion
3. **Cold brew** (a jar you already have) if you want cold coffee in summer
4. Siphon is optional and for enthusiasts

## Practical exercise

Choose two methods from this lesson and prepare the same coffee with each one:

1. **French press (Hoffmann technique):** 15g coffee, 250g boiling water, 9 minutes total
2. **Clever Dripper:** 15g coffee, 250g water at 95°C, 3 minutes immersion + drawdown

Taste them side by side. The French press will have more body and texture. The Clever will be cleaner and more defined. The difference shows you exactly what the paper filter does — it separates oils and fines, revealing acidity and clarity at the cost of body.

## Key concepts from this lesson

- French press = pure immersion. Hoffmann's technique (9 min, don't press hard) produces a much cleaner cup
- Clever Dripper = immersion + paper filter. Maximum consistency, minimum effort. Perfect for starting or evaluating coffees
- Cold brew extracts cold for 16-20 hours. Sweet and smooth but loses complexity — don't waste premium coffee here
- Siphon is more theater than technique — excellent cup but not superior to simpler methods
- Each method is a tool. There's no "best" — there's the right one for each coffee, moment, and preference
- The real barista skill is knowing which to use and why
