## The brewer that shouldn't work

The Aeropress was invented in 2005 by Alan Adler, an engineer from Stanford known for creating the Aerobie (a long-distance frisbee). He didn't come from the coffee world. He designed the Aeropress to solve a simple problem: make a good cup of individual coffee, quickly, without complications.

The result is a plastic cylinder with a rubber plunger that looks like a giant syringe. It's not elegant. It's not expensive ($30-40 USD). And yet it produces coffee that regularly competes with methods that cost ten times more. It has its own world championship (the World Aeropress Championship) where professional baristas present increasingly creative and unconventional recipes.

Why does it work so well? Because it combines two extraction methods: **immersion** (the coffee soaks in water, like in French press) and **pressure** (the plunger forces water through a filter). This combination extracts compounds that immersion alone can't reach, but more gently than espresso. The result is coffee with body, clean (thanks to the paper filter), and surprisingly versatile.

## Standard method

The standard method is how the Aeropress was designed to be used: the cylinder sits on the cup with the filter facing down, you pour water, stir, and press.

**Dose:** 15g of coffee
**Water:** 200g at 85-90°C (lower than V60 or Chemex)
**Grind:** Medium-fine (similar to table salt, slightly finer than pour-over)
**Total time:** ~2:00 minutes

### Step by step

**Setup:** Place a paper filter in the cap, rinse it with hot water. Screw the cap onto the cylinder. Set the cylinder on your cup or server with the "4" facing up.

**0:00 — Pour and stir:** Add 15g of ground coffee. Pour 200g of water in 10-15 seconds. Stir 5-10 times with the included agitator (or a spoon) to ensure complete saturation.

**0:30 — Insert plunger:** Place the plunger on top of the cylinder (without pressing) to create a seal that prevents water from dripping prematurely.

**1:00 — Press:** After 1 minute of immersion, press the plunger down slowly and steadily. Total pressure should take 20-30 seconds. When you hear an air hiss, stop — all the water has gone through.

### The problem with standard method

There's a detail: from the moment you pour water, some starts dripping through the filter by gravity. This means the first drops extract for less time than the last ones, creating slightly uneven extraction. For short recipes (1-2 minutes), the difference is minimal. But if you want longer immersions, this becomes a problem — and that's where the inverted method comes in.

## Inverted method

The inverted method solves premature dripping: you flip the Aeropress upside down (plunger down, opening up) so water can't escape until you decide.

**Dose:** 15g of coffee
**Water:** 200g at 90-95°C (you can use higher temperature than standard)
**Grind:** Medium (one step coarser than standard method)
**Total time:** ~2:30 minutes

### Step by step

**Setup:** Insert the plunger into the cylinder up to the "4" mark (just ~1cm). Flip everything — the plunger is down, the open end is up. Prepare the rinsed filter in the cap separately.

**0:00 — Coffee and water:** Add ground coffee. Pour 200g of water. Stir 3-5 times.

**0:15 — Wait:** Let the coffee extract by immersion. No dripping, no rush.

**2:00 — Prepare flip:** Screw on the cap with the filter (rinsed). Here comes the part that takes confidence: with a firm, decisive motion, flip the Aeropress onto your cup. Do it quickly — if you hesitate, it can spill.

**2:10 — Press:** Press slowly and steadily for 20-30 seconds.

### Which is better?

Neither one — they're different tools. The standard method is faster, simpler, and less risky (no flipping with hot water). The inverted gives you total control of immersion time and allows for more creative recipes. Most championship recipes use the inverted method for this reason.

## Temperature: lower than you'd expect

One of the most interesting features of the Aeropress is that it works exceptionally well with lower temperatures than other methods. Alan Adler originally recommended 80°C — much lower than the 92-96°C of pour-over.

Why? The combination of prolonged immersion + plunger pressure + fine grind compensates for the lower temperature. You extract enough compounds without the thermal aggressiveness that can pull out bitterness.

That said, temperature is the most experimental variable in Aeropress:

**75-80°C:** Very gentle, low extraction. Highlights sweetness and delicate notes. Works with light roasts of high quality where you want to preserve fruity acidity without any bitterness.

**80-85°C:** The classic Aeropress range. Medium body, pronounced sweetness, controlled acidity. Excellent for most coffees.

**85-92°C:** More extraction, more complexity. Good for medium and dark roasts that need more energy to reveal their flavors.

**92-100°C:** Freshly boiled water. Some championship recipes use boiling water with coarse grind and short time — the result can be surprisingly clean and bright.

The recommendation: experiment. The Aeropress is cheap, fast, and uses little coffee. You can make 3-4 brews in 20 minutes testing different temperatures with the same coffee.

## Championship recipes

The World Aeropress Championship (WAC) has been held since 2008 and produced some of the most creative recipes in specialty coffee. Each year, competitors push the Aeropress beyond its apparent limits. These recipes are public and worth trying.

### Patterns that repeat in winning recipes

Analyzing winning recipes from recent years, clear trends emerge:

**Post-extraction dilution (bypass).** Many champions prepare a concentrate with less water (100-120g) then dilute with hot water to final volume. This gives you a clean, balanced cup with the intensity of a concentrated immersion. It's basically an "Americano" made with Aeropress.

**Fine grind + short time.** Counter-intuitively, several winning recipes use nearly espresso-fine grind with 60-90 second immersions. The plunger pressure compensates for what would normally be over-extraction.

**Controlled agitation.** Stirring with precision (exact number of circles, direction, speed) is constant. Agitation is the "secret" variable in Aeropress — small changes produce noticeable differences.

**Unconventional water.** Some competitors use water mineralized specifically for their recipe — with mineral composition optimized for their coffee.

### Championship-style recipe (starting point)

**Dose:** 18g of coffee
**Water:** 90g at 85°C + 110g to dilute after
**Grind:** Fine (between espresso and pour-over)
**Method:** Inverted

**0:00:** Add coffee, pour 90g of water
**0:10:** Stir 5 times in one direction, 5 times in the other
**0:45:** Place cap, flip, press slowly (30 seconds)
**1:15:** Add 110g of hot water to the concentrate

The result is a clean, complex coffee with a sweetness that surprises for such a quick method.

## Filters: paper vs. metal

The Aeropress comes with disposable paper filters, but reusable metal filters exist (like Able Disk or Fellow Prismo). The difference is significant.

**Paper filter:** Traps oils and fines. Produces a clean, bright cup with light to medium body. More like a pour-over. It's the standard and what most championship recipes use.

**Metal filter:** Lets oils and micro-particles through. Produces a cup with more body, texture, and complexity — more like French press but without the coarse sediment. You lose some clarity for richness.

**Fellow Prismo:** A special accessory combining a metal filter with a pressure valve. Creates more resistance during pressing, bringing the result closer to something espresso-like (with crema included, though it's not real espresso crema). Interesting to experiment with, but not a replacement for espresso.

**Double paper:** Some baristas stack two paper filters to maximize cup cleanliness. The resulting coffee is ultra-clean, almost like tea. Works well with delicate light-roast coffees.

## Aeropress as a laboratory

Beyond being a brewing method, the Aeropress is the perfect tool for training your palate and understanding extraction variables. Why?

**It's fast.** One brew takes 2-3 minutes. You can make multiple in one session.

**Uses little coffee.** With 12-18g per brew, you can experiment without exhausting a whole bag.

**Isolates variables.** Its mechanical simplicity (no pump, no gravity-controlled flow) lets you change one variable and clearly feel the effect.

**It's reproducible.** There's no equipment variability — all Aeropress units are identical. If something changes, it was your technique.

Exercises you can do with Aeropress to train your palate:

Brew the same coffee three times changing only temperature (75°C, 85°C, 95°C). Taste side by side. This trains your palate to identify what temperature does.

Brew the same coffee three times changing only grind (fine, medium, coarse). Keep everything else the same. Feel the difference between under-extraction and over-extraction.

Brew once with paper filter and once with metal filter. Compare texture, clarity, and body.

## Aeropress Go and Aeropress Clear

Besides the original Aeropress, there are two variants:

**Aeropress Go:** Compact version designed for travel. Comes with a cup that functions as a case. Slightly smaller capacity (maximum ~200ml vs ~250ml of original). Same extraction quality, more portable.

**Aeropress Clear:** The newest version (2023), made of clear Tritan instead of the opaque polypropylene original. Visually more attractive, slightly more durable, same functionality. All three are compatible with the same filters and accessories.

## What you need for this lesson

- Aeropress (any version, ~$30-40 USD)
- Paper filters included
- A kettle (doesn't need to be gooseneck, though it helps)
- A scale with timer
- 15-18g of fresh coffee

## Practical exercise

Prepare the same coffee with all three approaches:

1. **Standard method:** 15g coffee, 200g water at 85°C, medium-fine grind, 1:30 total
2. **Inverted method:** 15g coffee, 200g water at 90°C, medium grind, 2:30 total
3. **Championship-style:** 18g coffee, 90g water at 85°C fine grind + 110g water to dilute

Taste them side by side. The standard will be the most gentle. The inverted will have more body. The concentrate-diluted one will likely have more complexity. Note which you prefer and think about why — whether temperature, contact time, or concentration changed your experience.

## Key concepts from this lesson

- Aeropress combines immersion + pressure, producing coffee unique between pour-over and espresso
- Standard method: fast and simple, but dripping limits immersion time
- Inverted method: total time control, ideal for creative recipes
- Works well with lower temperatures (80-85°C) thanks to plunger pressure
- Championship recipes use concentrate + dilution, fine grind, and precise agitation
- Paper filter = cleanliness; metal filter = body and texture
- It's the perfect lab for experimenting: fast, cheap, reproducible
- There's no single "correct" method — Aeropress rewards experimentation
