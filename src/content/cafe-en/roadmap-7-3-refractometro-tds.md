## Measuring the invisible

In Phase 1 we discussed TDS (Total Dissolved Solids) and extraction yield as theoretical concepts. You learned that ideal extraction is between 18-22%, espresso TDS hovers around 8-12%, and filtered coffee has TDS of 1.2-1.5%. But how are these numbers actually measured in practice?

The answer is the refractometer — an optical instrument that measures the concentration of dissolved solids in a liquid. Scott Rao was instrumental in bringing refractometer use from quality control labs to espresso bars. In his book "Espresso Extraction: Measurement and Mastery", he details exactly how to use it to diagnose and optimize extraction.

The refractometer is not a luxury or a gadget — it's a diagnostic tool that tells you objectively what's happening in your cup. Combined with your palate, it converts the dialing-in process from guesswork to science.

## What is TDS and what the refractometer measures

### TDS (Total Dissolved Solids)

TDS is the percentage of dissolved coffee solids in the water. If your espresso has a TDS of 10%, it means 10% of the liquid in the cup is dissolved coffee and 90% is water.

**Typical espresso:** TDS 8-12%
**Typical filtered coffee:** TDS 1.15-1.55%
**Immersion coffee (French press):** TDS 1.0-1.3%

### Extraction yield

Extraction yield is the percentage of total solubles from the dry coffee that ended up in the cup. A coffee bean has ~30% extractable solubles. If you extract 20% of them, your yield is 20%.

**Formula:**
Extraction (%) = (Beverage weight × TDS) / Dry coffee weight × 100

**Example:** 18g coffee → 36g espresso with 10% TDS
Extraction = (36 × 0.10) / 18 × 100 = 20%

### The TDS ↔ Extraction relationship

TDS and extraction are connected but different:

- **High TDS + low extraction** = Concentrated but under-extracted shot (short ratio, like a ristretto). Intense but sour and lacking sweetness.
- **Low TDS + high extraction** = Diluted but well-extracted shot (very long ratio). Sweet but watery.
- **Medium TDS + medium extraction** = The sweet spot. Concentrated enough with sufficient extraction.

Moving the ratio changes both: a longer ratio (more water) lowers TDS but raises extraction. Moving the grind changes extraction without changing ratio: finer grind = higher extraction = higher TDS.

## The refractometer: how it works

A refractometer measures the refractive index of a liquid — how much a light ray bends passing through it. Dissolved solids change the refractive index proportionally to their concentration. The instrument converts that optical measurement to a TDS percentage.

### Which one to buy

**VST LAB Coffee III (~$700 USD):** The industry standard. Designed specifically for coffee, automatic temperature compensation, ±0.01% precision. It's expensive but it's what professionals and competitors use.

**Atago PAL-Coffee (~$350 USD):** More accessible alternative. Designed for coffee, good precision. Sufficient for most non-competitive uses.

**Generic refractometers (~$30-100):** Measure Brix (sugar) not coffee TDS. You can make approximate conversions but they're not ideal. If this is your budget, it's better than nothing.

## The Rao three-drop technique

Rao describes a specific technique for measuring TDS that maximizes precision:

### The temperature problem

The refractometer has a temperature sensor that compensates for differences, but hot coffee evaporates solvents from the sample while you're measuring, changing the TDS. Also, a hot drop on a cold prism creates a temperature gradient that distorts the reading.

### Rao's solution

**1. Stir the sample.** Before taking the sample, stir the coffee to homogenize it. TDS can vary between the surface and bottom of the cup.

**2. Let it cool.** Wait for the sample to drop to room temperature (or at least <40°C). You can speed this by putting a teaspoon of coffee on a plate and waiting 1-2 minutes.

**3. Only 3 drops.** Place exactly 3 drops (with a dropper or pipette) on the refractometer prism. More liquid = longer reading time = more evaporation = less precision.

**4. Wait 30 seconds.** Let the sample stabilize on the prism before taking the reading.

**5. Read and record.** Note the TDS. Clean the prism with distilled water and dry it before the next measurement.

**6. Measure twice.** To verify, repeat the measurement. If the two readings differ by more than 0.05%, something went wrong — clean and repeat.

### Common mistakes

**Measuring hot coffee.** Evaporation skews the reading upward (artificially high TDS).

**Unhomogenized sample.** If you didn't stir, the sample may have different TDS than the cup average.

**Dirty prism.** Residue from previous measurements or fingerprints alter the reading. Clean with distilled water between each measurement.

**Excess sample.** More liquid on the prism = more thermal and evaporation variability.

## The Brewing Control Chart

The SCA developed a graph called the "Brewing Control Chart" (also known as "Coffee Brewing Chart") that maps TDS against extraction. Rao uses this chart extensively.

### How to read it

The X-axis is extraction yield (%). The Y-axis is concentration (TDS %). In the center is a rectangular zone representing the "ideal range" according to the SCA:

**Extraction:** 18-22%
**TDS (filtered):** 1.15-1.55%

Each point on the chart represents a specific preparation. If your point falls within the ideal zone, your coffee is "well-extracted and at the correct concentration." If it falls outside:

**Top-left:** Over-concentrated and under-extracted. Intense but sour. Solution: more water (longer ratio) or finer grind.

**Bottom-right:** Sub-concentrated and over-extracted. Bitter and watery. Solution: less water or coarser grind.

**Top-right:** Over-concentrated and over-extracted. Intense and bitter. Solution: more water AND coarser grind.

**Bottom-left:** Sub-concentrated and under-extracted. Watery and sour. Solution: less water AND finer grind.

### Rao's perspective on the chart

Rao respects the Brewing Control Chart but warns it's not gospel. Some exceptional coffees taste better at 23-24% extraction — outside the "ideal" range. The chart is a starting point, not a law. Your palate always has the final word.

## Measuring vs tasting: Rao's balance

Rao emphasizes a point many technical baristas forget: the refractometer doesn't replace your palate. It complements it.

**When measuring is more useful than tasting:**
- When training new staff and you need an objective standard
- When diagnosing a problem (is it under-extraction or low concentration?)
- When switching coffees and you need a starting point
- When verifying consistency between baristas or between shifts

**When tasting is more useful than measuring:**
- When deciding if a coffee tastes good (TDS can be "correct" but flavor isn't)
- When exploring outside standard ranges
- When the coffee has defects (the refractometer won't detect flavor defects)

**The ideal workflow according to Rao:**
1. Prepare a shot/cup
2. Taste. Do you like it?
3. If not, measure. Do TDS and extraction tell you what to adjust?
4. Adjust one variable. Prepare again.
5. Taste again. Did it improve?
6. Measure to confirm and document the recipe.

The refractometer speeds up dialing-in by eliminating guesswork. Instead of changing variables randomly, you know exactly where you are on the extraction map and can move in the right direction.

## Practical application: dialing in with a refractometer

### For espresso

1. **Base shot:** 18g coffee, medium espresso grind, 1:2 ratio (36g out), ~25-30 sec
2. **Measure:** TDS should be 8-12%. Calculate extraction.
3. **If extraction <18%:** Grind finer. TDS and extraction will both rise.
4. **If extraction >22%:** Grind coarser. Both will fall.
5. **If TDS okay but extraction low:** Increase ratio (to 1:2.5 or 1:3).
6. **Taste.** If it tastes good in your extraction range, document the recipe.

### For filtered coffee

1. **Base preparation:** 15g coffee, 250g water, your preferred method
2. **Measure:** TDS should be 1.15-1.55%. Calculate extraction.
3. **If under-extracted (low TDS, <18%):** Grind finer or increase agitation.
4. **If over-extracted (high TDS, >22%):** Grind coarser or reduce time.
5. **Document** the winning recipe.

## Practical exercise

If you have access to a refractometer:

1. Prepare 3 shots with different grinds (fine, medium, coarse) at the same ratio (1:2)
2. Measure the TDS of each
3. Calculate extraction for each
4. Taste all three and note which you prefer
5. Compare your preference with the numbers: did the one you liked most have the highest extraction? Or do you prefer extraction different from the SCA "ideal"?

If you don't have a refractometer, the exercise is still valuable without measurement. The difference is you'll have to trust your palate more to diagnose — which is also a skill.

## Key concepts from this lesson

- TDS measures solids concentration; extraction yield measures what percentage of coffee was extracted
- The refractometer turns dialing-in from guesswork to science
- Rao's technique: 3 drops at room temperature, wait 30 sec, measure twice
- The Brewing Control Chart maps TDS vs extraction; the ideal zone is 18-22% extraction
- Measuring complements your palate, it doesn't replace it: flavor always has the final word
- Extractions outside the "ideal" range can be delicious — the chart is a guide, not law
- The workflow: taste → measure → adjust → taste → document
