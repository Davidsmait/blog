## Beyond the basics

If you master the heart, rosetta and tulip, you have the tools to build practically any latte art design. Advanced patterns don't introduce new movements — they combine, modify and chain basic movements in increasingly complex ways.

This lesson covers three areas: pattern combinations, competition designs, and etching (needle drawing). This is where latte art transitions from technical skill to a form of expression.

## Pattern combinations

### Rosetta within tulip

One of the most popular designs in specialty cafés. Combines the stacked structure of the tulip with the detail of the rosetta.

**Technique:** Make a 3-4 layer tulip as you already know. But on the last layer (closest to you), instead of simply depositing a circle, make a short rosetta wiggle. Finish with the forward cut through everything.

The result is a tulip where the "top layer" has fern leaves while the lower layers are solid tulip pushes. It's visually more interesting than either pattern alone.

### Swan

The swan is a rosetta whose final cut curves to form the neck and head of a swan. It's the first "figurative" design most baristas learn.

**Technique:** Make a normal rosetta but stop before reaching the cup's front — leave space. For the cut, instead of cutting straight, draw an "S" curve: up, curve right (or left), and end with a small dot that forms the swan's head.

The difficulty is the neck: you need a very thin flow and precise wrist movement for the curve to be smooth and not angular. Practice the "S" movement in the air with an empty pitcher before trying it with milk.

### Double tulip (stacked tulip)

Two parallel tulips in the same cup. Requires a wide cup and good spatial control.

**Technique:** Make a small tulip (2-3 layers) on one side of the cup. Then rotate the cup and make another tulip on the other side. Both tulips' cuts meet in the center.

Spatial coordination is the challenge: if the first tulip is too large, there's no room for the second. Practice with large cups (350ml+) first.

### Multi-rosetta

Several small rosettas organized in a radial pattern, like flower petals.

**Technique:** Each rosetta is made individually, rotating the cup between one and the next. Typically 3-5 small rosettas around a central point. It's a design you see in competitions but rarely in daily service because it takes too long.

## Advanced pouring techniques

### Contrast control

The contrast between milk's white and crema's brown is what makes latte art visually striking. There are techniques to maximize it:

**Clean integration.** During the integration phase, pour with precision to not "stain" the crema prematurely. A thin, centered stream mixes below without leaving white marks on the surface.

**Dense crema.** Espresso with thick, stable crema retains contrast better. Medium roasts with good CO2 levels (fresh coffee, 7-21 days post-roast) produce the best crema.

**Execution speed.** The faster you complete the pattern once you start depositing, the sharper the contrast. Crema degrades over time — if you take too long, the brown dilutes and everything becomes beige.

### Symmetry

Symmetry is what separates "good" latte art from "excellent." In competitions, symmetry is evaluated with millimeter precision.

**Centering.** The pattern should be centered in the cup. Practice positioning your insertion point exactly on the central axis.

**Lateral balance.** In a rosetta, leaves on both sides should be the same size. This depends on your wiggle having the same amplitude left and right. If you're right-handed, you probably have a natural bias toward one side — identify it and consciously correct it.

**Proportion.** The pattern should fill the cup harmoniously — neither so small it disappears nor so large it deforms against the edges.

### Variable speed

Competition baristas modulate flow speed during the pattern:

**Slow start.** The first leaves of a rosetta are made with moderate flow to establish the pattern with precision.

**Gradual acceleration.** As you retreat, you can slightly increase flow to keep leaves the same size (since leaves closer to you are deposited over a wider cup area).

**Controlled finish.** The cut requires an abrupt flow reduction — from generous to a thin stream in an instant.

## Etching: drawing with a tool

Etching (or needle latte art) is a different technique from free pouring. After pouring a base of milk (usually a white canvas or simple pattern), you use a fine tool (etching needle, toothpick, thermometer) to draw on the surface.

### Etching tools

**Etching needle (latte art pen):** A fine needle with a handle, similar to a poke tool. It's the standard tool. Available at barista supply stores for $5-15.

**Toothpick:** Works but is short and hard to control. Only for emergencies.

**Thermometer:** The milk thermometer's probe can serve as an improvised tool.

**Squeeze bottle with chocolate or caramel:** To add sauce lines you can etch through.

### Basic etching technique

**Step 1 — The base:** Pour a latte with a uniform white surface (no free-pour pattern) or a simple heart.

**Step 2 — Sauce lines:** With a squeeze bottle of chocolate or caramel, draw parallel lines over the white surface. Lines should be thin and uniform. Usually 3-5 parallel lines.

**Step 3 — Drag:** With the needle, drag across the lines perpendicularly. If you drag top to bottom then bottom to top alternating, the lines distort creating geometric patterns (similar to marbled stone).

**Step 4 — Details:** Use the needle's tip to create fine details: small hearts, leaves, spirals, or figurative designs.

### Classic etching designs

**Spider web:** Concentric chocolate lines, then drag from center outward (and outward to center alternating). Creates a perfect spider web pattern.

**Chain hearts:** Chocolate dots aligned, then drag across them in one direction. Each dot deforms into a heart shape.

**Bear/animal:** A white base with ears drawn in chocolate (two dots at the top), eyes (two smaller dots), nose and mouth with needle drags. The animal designs in latte art you see on social media are almost all etching.

### Etching vs. free pouring

In specialty coffee culture, free pouring has more "respect" than etching. Why? Because free pouring is a real-time skill — you can't pause or correct. Etching allows corrections and doesn't require milk flow control.

In latte art competitions, free pouring and etching are evaluated in separate categories. Free pouring usually carries more weight in overall scoring.

That said, there's no reason not to do both. Etching lets you create designs impossible with free pouring (faces, animals, text), and combining free pouring with etching produces the most impressive results.

## Latte art in competition

The World Latte Art Championship (WLAC) evaluates baristas on three beverages: a free-pour pattern, a combination design (free pour + etching), and a "designer drink" where total creativity is evaluated.

### Evaluation criteria

**Visual symmetry:** Is the design symmetrical on all axes? Evaluated with precision.

**Contrast:** Is the white white and brown brown? Sharp contrast indicates technical control.

**Definition:** Are lines clean and edges sharp? Blurry lines indicate deficient microfoam.

**Complexity:** Does the design demonstrate technical difficulty? An 8-layer tulip is more complex than a 3-layer.

**Creativity:** Is it original? In the designer drink, originality of concept matters.

**Clean cup:** Are there splashes or marks outside the design? The overall cup presentation is evaluated.

### Competition trends

Competitions have evolved enormously. In the early years, a clean rosetta could win. Today, competitors create designs with 15+ tulip layers, microscopic rosettas within each layer, and combinations that seem impossible.

Some recent trends: deliberately asymmetrical designs (breaking the tradition of perfect symmetry), use of negative space (brown crema as a design element, not just background), and multi-flow patterns (pouring from two pitchers simultaneously).

## Deliberate practice: how to improve fast

### The 100-repetition method

An approach used by competition baristas: choose a single pattern and do it 100 times in one week (14-15 per day). The first 30 will be bad. From 30 to 70, you'll feel consistency emerging. From 70 to 100, you're refining details. At week's end, that pattern will be automatic.

### Photography and analysis

Take a photo of each attempt. At session's end, compare photos and look for error patterns. Is the problem symmetry? Contrast? Leaf definition? Identifying the specific error lets you focus on fixing it.

### Practice without espresso

If you don't want to prepare 15 espressos daily, there are alternatives. Strong black tea works as a visual base (no crema but you can see the milk). Some baristas practice with hot water tinted with food coloring. It's not identical, but it lets you repeat pouring mechanics without wasting coffee.

### The wrist, not the arm

The most common mistake in intermediate baristas is moving the whole arm instead of the wrist. The rosetta's wiggle comes from the wrist — a short, quick, relaxed movement. If you move your elbow or shoulder, the movement is too wide and imprecise.

Try this: rest your elbow on a table and make the wiggling movement with just your wrist. That's the range of motion you need. Now reproduce that standing with the pitcher over the cup.

## Practical exercise

**Session 1 — Combinations (45 min):** Prepare 5 lattes with combinations. Try a rosetta within a tulip, then a basic swan. Don't seek perfection — seek to understand the mechanics of combining movements.

**Session 2 — Etching (30 min):** Prepare 3 lattes with a clean white surface. Practice chocolate spider web with needle drag. Then try chain hearts. Finally, try a free design.

**Session 3 — Analysis (15 min):** Review photos from all sessions this week. Identify your most common error and dedicate the next session exclusively to fixing it.

## Key concepts from this lesson

- Advanced patterns are combinations of three basic movements (deposit, wiggle, push)
- The swan is a rosetta with an "S" curve cut — your first figurative design
- Contrast, symmetry and definition are the three quality criteria for latte art
- Etching uses needle and sauces to create designs impossible with free pouring (animals, text, faces)
- In competition, free pouring is weighted more than etching
- The 100-repetition method in one week is the fastest way to master a pattern
- Movement comes from the wrist, not the arm
- Photograph and analyze each attempt — visual feedback accelerates learning
