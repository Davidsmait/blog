## The Chemex: design that defines flavor

The Chemex was invented in 1941 by chemist Peter Schlumbohm. Its borosilicate glass and wood design is in the Museum of Modern Art's permanent collection — it's one of the few coffee makers considered a work of art. But what makes it special isn't the aesthetics; it's the filter.

The Chemex filter is 20-30% thicker than a V60 or Kalita filter. This difference changes everything: the filter absorbs more oils and retains more fines, producing an exceptionally clean, bright cup with light body. If espresso is an oil painting, the Chemex is a watercolor — each note stands apart with clarity.

## The filter: your hidden variable

Chemex filters come in two versions: square (folded) and circular (pre-folded). Both work the same in the cup, but square filters are easier to find.

How to fold the square filter: fold it in half twice to form a quarter-circle. Open the side with three layers facing the spout of the Chemex (where air comes out). One layer faces the other side. This creates an air channel so water flows without creating a vacuum.

**Chemex filters vs. generic filters:** Generic Chemex filters are noticeably thinner. The result is a cup with more body and more oils — more like a V60 than a classic Chemex. If you want the authentic Chemex experience, use the original brand filters. If you prefer more body, experiment with thinner filters.

**Rinse the filter:** Always rinse the filter with hot water before brewing. This does two things: removes paper taste (which is real, especially in white filters) and preheats the Chemex. Discard the rinse water through the spout.

## Base recipe: the one that always works

This is a reliable recipe for a 6-cup Chemex you can use as a starting point:

**Dose:** 30g of coffee
**Water:** 500g at 93-95°C
**Ratio:** 1:16.7
**Grind:** Medium — like kosher salt. Slightly coarser than for V60 because the thicker filter already slows flow.
**Target total time:** 4:00 - 4:30 minutes

### Pouring structure

**0:00 — Bloom:** Pour 60g of water in gentle circles, making sure to wet all the coffee. Give the Chemex a gentle swirl to level. Wait 45 seconds.

**0:45 — First pour:** Pour slowly until you reach 300g total. Start from the center in a spiral outward, without touching the filter walls. Maintain constant, controlled flow. This should take about 30-40 seconds.

**1:25 — Wait:** Let the level drop to about half the coffee bed. About 20-30 seconds.

**1:55 — Second pour:** Pour to 500g total with the same circular pattern. A gentle swirl at the end to level the bed.

**Drawdown:** Water should finish dripping between 3:45 and 4:30. If it takes longer, grind coarser. If it finishes before 3:30, grind finer.

## Single-pour recipe (simpler)

If you prefer something more straightforward:

**0:00 — Bloom:** 60g of water, 45 seconds.
**0:45 — Continuous pour:** Pour slowly and steadily to 500g. Keep uniform flow, spiraling from center. Don't stop. Swirl at the end.
**Total time:** 3:30 - 4:15.

This technique is simpler and produces very consistent results because you reduce variables — you only have to maintain constant flow.

## Common Chemex mistakes

**Grinding too fine:** The thick filter already slows flow. If you also grind fine, drawdown can stretch past 6 minutes and your coffee will be over-extracted, bitter, and astringent. When in doubt, start coarser.

**Pouring on the filter walls:** If you pour directly on the paper filter, water runs down the walls without passing through the coffee — pure bypass. Always pour on the coffee bed, keeping a margin of 1-2 cm from the walls.

**Not rinsing the filter:** Paper taste is subtle but real, especially with unbleached white filters. A quick rinse with hot water eliminates it.

**Old coffee or pre-ground:** The Chemex, with its clarity, amplifies all defects. If the coffee is past its prime or was ground hours ago, you'll notice flat flavors, cardboard, and lack of complexity that in a method with more body (like French press) would be more hidden.

**Leaving coffee in the Chemex after brewing:** The glass retains heat and keeps extracting. If you leave the coffee with the filter on, it cools slowly while over-extracting. Remove the filter immediately after drawdown finishes.

## The Chemex cup profile

The Chemex produces coffee with very defined characteristics: light to medium body, bright and defined acidity, subtle sweetness, clean and prolonged aftertaste, and a transparency that lets you distinguish individual notes.

Coffees that shine in Chemex: washed Ethiopians (jasmine, citrus, bergamot), Kenyans (currant, tomato, juicy acidity), high-altitude washed Colombias (caramel, apple, brown sugar), coffees with washed processing in general.

Coffees that may not work as well: very fermented naturals (the Chemex can make them taste too "wild" without the body that contains them), very dark roasts (the Chemex's clarity exposes charcoal bitterness), Robusta (needs the body of other methods).

## Caring for your Chemex

Borosilicate glass is heat-resistant but fragile to impacts. Wash your Chemex with warm water and gentle soap after each use. Coffee oils adhere to the glass and over time form a film that affects flavor.

For deep cleaning: fill it with hot water and a tablespoon of baking soda. Let soak 30 minutes, rinse well. Coffee stains on the glass come off with a bottle brush.

Don't use steel wool or abrasive cleaners — they scratch the glass.

## Practical exercise

Prepare two Chemex cups of the same coffee with the same recipe but different grinds:

1. **Medium-fine grind** (like sand)
2. **Medium-coarse grind** (like kosher salt)

Note the drawdown times and the flavor of each one. The first should be more intense and potentially bitter; the second lighter and potentially more acidic. Which do you prefer? Your preference tells you which direction to adjust.
