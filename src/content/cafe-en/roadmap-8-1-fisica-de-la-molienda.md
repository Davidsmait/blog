## Your grinder is your most important instrument

In previous lessons we talked about grinding in practical terms: finer for espresso, coarser for French press, adjust if it tastes sour or bitter. That's enough to make good coffee. But if you want to understand *why* certain grinders produce cleaner coffee, why the same recipe tastes different with another grinder, or why high-end grinders cost what they do, you need to understand the physics of what happens when a coffee bean breaks apart.

Jonathan Gagné, an astrophysicist and author of *The Physics of Filter Coffee*, applies material mechanics and statistical analysis to grinding. This lesson translates those concepts into practical knowledge.

## Mechanical properties of the bean

Roasted coffee is a **brittle** material: when sufficient force is applied, it breaks suddenly rather than deforming gradually. This is different from ductile materials like copper, which stretch before breaking.

### Brittle fracture

When grinder burrs apply force to a bean, it doesn't compress smoothly — it fractures along lines of structural weakness. These lines are determined by the grain's cell structure, the porosity created during roasting (more roasting = more porous = more brittle), and pre-existing microfractures.

The result is that each bean doesn't break into uniformly-sized particles. It breaks into a range of sizes: large pieces, medium pieces, and fine powder. This distribution of sizes is the most important factor in your grinding quality.

### Effect of roast level

A darker roast makes the bean more porous and brittle. It breaks more easily, with less force, but also produces more fines (powder). A lighter roast is denser and more resistant — it requires more force but produces a more uniform particle distribution.

This has practical implications: if you switch from a light roast coffee to a dark one without adjusting your grinder, it's not just the flavor that changes — the particle distribution changes, which changes how water flows through it, which changes extraction in ways beyond the roast profile alone.

## Particle distribution

### What it is and why it matters

Particle distribution describes how many particles of each size your grinder produces. A "perfect" grinder would produce all particles the same size. In reality, every grinder produces a range — the question is how wide that range is.

### Bimodal distribution

Most coffee grinders produce a **bimodal** distribution: two peaks in the size graph. One main peak at the "target" size (the grind you selected) and a second peak of fines — very small particles generated as a byproduct of brittle fracture.

Gagné explains that these fines aren't simply "the same coffee smaller." Fines extract much faster than large particles because they have more surface area relative to volume. In a cup, fines might be over-extracted (bitter, astringent) while large particles are under-extracted (sour, weak). The resulting flavor is an average of both extremes — not a clean middle ground.

### Unimodal distribution

The best grinders on the market (like the Mahlkönig EK43, or grinders with high-precision burrs) produce more **unimodal** distributions: a single dominant peak with very few fines. This means more uniform extraction, cleaner flavor, and more sweetness because you're not averaging over and under-extraction.

The difference between a $200 grinder and a $2000 grinder isn't that one grinds "finer" or "coarser" — it's that the expensive one produces a narrower, more uniform particle distribution.

## Grinder anatomy

### Types of burrs

**Flat burrs:** Two parallel disks with teeth. Coffee enters the center and exits the edges. They produce more unimodal distributions than conical, especially at large diameters (64mm+). The flavor tends to be cleaner and more articulate.

**Conical burrs:** An inner cone rotating inside an outer ring. Coffee moves through gravity and centrifugal force. They produce more bimodal distributions (more fines) but with different character — often described as more "body" and texture. They're more energy-efficient and generate less heat.

**Roller mills:** Used in commercial and industrial coffee. Two cylinders rotating in opposite directions. Can produce very uniform distributions but require specific speeds and pressures.

### Alignment

Burr alignment is critical. If burrs aren't perfectly parallel (flat burrs) or concentric (conical), one side of the grinder grinds finer than the other. This artificially widens particle distribution — even excellent burrs produce mediocre results if misaligned.

Gagné documents how alignment differences of just 50 microns (0.05mm) are detectable in the cup. Coffee enthusiasts measure alignment with aluminum foil or markers to verify that burrs make contact uniformly across their entire circumference.

### Materials

**Steel:** The standard. Durable, sharp, economical. Wears with use (typically after 500-1000 kg of coffee), which gradually broadens particle distribution.

**Titanium-coated steel:** Greater durability, holds its edge longer. Reduces heat generation from friction.

**Ceramic:** Extremely hard, doesn't transfer heat, doesn't retain odors. But it's brittle — can chip if it encounters a stone or foreign object in the coffee.

### RPM and heat

Burr speed affects grinding. Higher RPM generates more heat from friction, which can affect volatile compounds in the coffee. Slow (low-RPM) grinders preserve more aromatics but grind more slowly.

Gagné points out that the effect of heat on grinding has been exaggerated — in most home scenarios, the difference is minimal. Where it matters is in high-volume coffee shops where the grinder runs continuously and heat accumulates significantly.

## Retention and the exchange problem

### What is retention

Every grinder retains some coffee between the burrs, the chamber, and the outlet. When you grind your next dose, part of what comes out is old (retained) coffee mixed with fresh coffee. This is called "exchange" or interchange.

### Why it matters

Retained coffee oxidizes and stales faster because it's already ground. Mixing old with fresh contaminates the flavor and reduces consistency. In coffee shops, where freshness is critical, retention is a decisive factor in grinder selection.

### Low-retention grinders

Grinders designed for low retention (like the Niche Zero, DF64, or single-dose grinders) minimize dead space. Some use fans or bellows to expel retained coffee. The best achieve retention under 0.1g — practically zero exchange.

## Sieving: the technique of obsessives

### The concept

Some professionals and competitors sieve their ground coffee with a specific-size sieve to remove fines. If your grinder produces a bimodal distribution, you can physically separate the fines with a 200-400 micron mesh.

### The result

Sieved coffee produces noticeably cleaner cups — more sweetness, less astringency, more defined flavors. It's like upgrading your grinder without buying a new one. Jonathan Gagné documents measurable improvements in extraction uniformity after sieving.

### The downside

You lose 10-20% of your ground coffee (the fines you discard). This increases cost per cup. It also adds an extra step to your workflow. That's why it's mainly used in competitions and cupping sessions, not daily service.

## The Kruve effect and particle measurement

### Measuring your distribution

Without measurement tools, particle distribution is invisible. Gagné uses image analysis (particle photography under microscope and counting software) to quantify distributions. For home use, calibrated sieve sets like Kruve allow an approximation: you sieve your grind through different-sized meshes and weigh what remains at each level.

### Interpreting the data

If most of your coffee stays on one sieve (for example, 600-800 microns), your distribution is narrow — good grinder. If it spreads across three or four sieves with similar amounts, your distribution is wide — less precise grinder or worn burrs.

## What you need for this lesson

- Your current grinder (any type)
- Light and dark roasted whole beans for comparison
- White sheet for observing grind
- Magnifying glass or phone camera zoom (to view particles)
- Optional: set of calibrated sieves

## Practical exercise

### Visual observation

1. Grind 15g of light roast at medium grind (pour-over setting)
2. Spread the grind on white paper
3. Observe with magnifier or phone zoom: do you see very different particle sizes? Is there fine dust visible among larger particles?
4. Repeat with dark roast at the same setting
5. Compare: which produced more visible fines?

### Retention comparison

1. Grind a dose of 18g
2. Weigh what comes out of the grinder
3. The difference is the retention
4. Gently tap the grinder and weigh what comes out additionally
5. Note your grinder's total retention

### Sieving test (if you have sieves)

1. Grind 20g at medium grind
2. Pass through a 400 micron sieve
3. Weigh what passed through (fines) and what remained on top
4. Prepare two identical cups: one with complete grind, one with only what remained on top (adjusting dose)
5. Compare flavor: is the sieved cup cleaner?

## Key concepts from this lesson

- Coffee is a brittle material that fractures into size ranges, not uniform particles
- Bimodal distribution (two size peaks) is the main problem of most grinders
- Unimodal distribution (single peak) produces more uniform extraction and cleaner flavor
- Flat burrs tend toward more uniform distributions than conical
- Burr alignment affects distribution as much as burr quality
- Retention mixes old coffee with fresh — single-dose grinders minimize this problem
- Sieving removes fines and improves the cup, but wastes coffee and adds complexity
- Understanding your grinder's physics lets you diagnose problems you previously attributed to other factors
