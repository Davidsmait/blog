## Why V60 is the standard

The Hario V60 is probably the most popular dripper in specialty coffee. Its name comes from its 60-degree conical angle. What makes it special: a single large hole at the bottom (no flow restriction) and internal spiral ridges that create air channels between the filter and the walls. This means you control the contact time entirely through your grind and pouring technique — the equipment doesn't dictate anything to you.

That freedom is its greatest virtue and its greatest challenge. The V60 is the instrument of the demanding barista: it amplifies good technique and exposes bad technique without mercy.

## The James Hoffmann technique

This is the technique Hoffmann shares on his channel and in his book. It's the modern standard for V60 and an excellent starting point.

**Dose:** 15g of coffee
**Water:** 250g at 95°C
**Grind:** Medium-fine (finer than Chemex, coarser than espresso — like fine beach sand)

### Step by step

**0:00 — Bloom:** Pour 50g of water quickly over all the coffee. Stir with a spoon or swirl the dripper to ensure complete saturation. Wait until 0:45.

**0:45 — Main pour:** Pour slowly and steadily in concentric circles, maintaining constant flow. Reach 150g around the 1:15 mark.

**1:15 — Continue pouring** slowly to 250g. You should reach 250g around 1:30-1:45.

**Finish:** Give the dripper a gentle swirl (the "Rao spin") to level the coffee bed and ensure uniform extraction during drawdown.

**Complete drawdown:** 2:30-3:30 total.

What makes this technique special: the aggressive bloom (with active stirring) ensures complete saturation, and continuous pouring without pauses maintains stable water level that produces predictable extraction.

## Tetsu Kasuya's 4:6 method

Tetsu Kasuya won the 2016 World Brewers Cup with this technique. It's radically different from the Hoffmann method and gives you explicit control over sweetness and strength.

The idea: you divide the total water into 5 pours of equal weight. The first 2 pours (the "40%") control the acid-sweet balance. The last 3 pours (the "60%") control strength/concentration.

**Dose:** 20g of coffee
**Water:** 300g at 92-93°C (lower than Hoffmann)
**Grind:** Coarse — coarser than you'd typically expect for V60. Kasuya uses noticeably coarser grind than standard.

### Structure

Each pour is 60g (300g total / 5 pours = 60g each). Wait 45 seconds between each pour.

**Pour 1 (0:00):** 60g — If you pour slower, you extract more acids.
**Pour 2 (0:45):** 60g — If you pour faster, you extract more sweetness.
**Pour 3 (1:30):** 60g
**Pour 4 (2:15):** 60g
**Pour 5 (3:00):** 60g

**Balance adjustment:** For more acidity, make the first pour larger (70g) and the second smaller (50g). For more sweetness, do the opposite.

**Strength adjustment:** For stronger coffee, divide the last 180g into 2 pours instead of 3. For lighter, into 4 pours.

What's interesting about 4:6 is that it decouples flavor balance from concentration — you can have sweet and light, or acidic and intense, just by changing the pouring structure.

## Comparison of techniques

| | Hoffmann | Kasuya 4:6 |
|---|---------|-----------|
| Grind | Medium-fine | Coarse |
| Temperature | 95°C | 92-93°C |
| Bloom | Stirred | Normal |
| Pours | Continuous (1-2 phases) | 5 phases of 45s |
| Control | Grind as main variable | Pouring structure as variable |
| Profile | Clean, balanced, complex | Flexible — acidic or sweet by pours |
| Difficulty | Medium | Low (more mechanical) |

There's no "better" technique. Hoffmann produces more consistent results with fewer decisions. Kasuya gives you more knobs to adjust but requires understanding what each pour does.

## V60-specific variables

**Dripper size:** V60 comes in 01 (1-2 cups, 1-15g), 02 (1-4 cups, 15-30g), and 03 (up to 6 cups). The 02 is the standard and most versatile.

**Material:** Plastic, ceramic, glass, metal, copper. Plastic is most recommended because it retains less heat (doesn't steal temperature from water) and is cheap. Ceramic absorbs heat during the first brew and can cause faster drawdowns on the first coffee of the day. Metal and copper conduct heat quickly but cool just as fast.

**Filters:** Hario white filters (tabbed or untabbed) are standard. Natural (unbleached, brown) filters have more pronounced paper taste — rinse them well. Third-party filters like Cafec (with abaca fiber) produce faster drawdowns and a slightly different cup.

**Drawdown speed:** On V60, if your drawdown is consistently very slow (more than 4 minutes for a 250g recipe), the fines from your grinder are probably clogging the filter. Options: grind coarser, use a better grinder, or try the "tap" technique — gently tap the dripper on the table after bloom to settle the fines before pouring.

## What you need for this lesson

- Hario V60 size 02 (plastic works perfectly, costs ~$8-12 USD)
- White Hario filters
- A gooseneck kettle
- A scale with timer
- 15-20g of fresh coffee

## Practical exercise

Prepare the same coffee with both techniques:

1. **Hoffmann:** 15g coffee, 250g water at 95°C, medium-fine grind
2. **Kasuya 4:6:** 20g coffee, 300g water at 92°C, coarse grind, 5 pours of 60g

Taste them side by side. The Hoffmann should be cleaner and more balanced. The Kasuya will likely have more body and different sweetness. Note which you prefer and why — this tells you a lot about your palate.

## Key concepts from this lesson

- V60 has a large hole with no restriction — you control everything with grind and pouring
- Hoffmann technique: stirred bloom, continuous pour, final swirl. Clean and consistent.
- Kasuya 4:6 method: 5 equal pours every 45s. The 40% controls balance, the 60% controls strength.
- Plastic is the best material because it doesn't steal heat
- Slow drawdown = fines clogging filter → grind coarser or upgrade your grinder
- V60 amplifies everything — good technique and good coffee shine; mistakes are exposed
