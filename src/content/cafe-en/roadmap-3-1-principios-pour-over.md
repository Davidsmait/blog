## Pour-over: total control

If espresso is pressure and velocity, pour-over is patience and precision. It's the method where you directly control every variable — how much water you pour, where you pour it, at what speed, and when. There's no pump or mechanical pressure. Just gravity, hot water, and your technique.

The result, when done well, is coffee with exceptional clarity: you can distinguish individual flavors that in espresso fuse into one dense whole. A washed Ethiopian on a V60 can reveal notes of jasmine, peach, and bergamot with a precision impossible to achieve in other methods.

## The bloom: the first critical phase

When hot water touches freshly ground coffee, the first thing that happens is a violent release of CO2 — the gas trapped during roasting. You see the coffee swell, bubble, and expand. This is the **bloom** (blooming).

Why does it matter? CO2 is hydrophobic — it repels water. If you pour all the water at once onto CO2-filled coffee, the gas creates barriers that prevent water from making uniform contact with the grounds. The result is uneven extraction.

Standard technique: pour twice the weight of water as your coffee dose (for example, 30g of water for 15g of coffee), making sure to fully saturate the coffee. Wait 30-45 seconds. You'll see the expansion and then the coffee will "deflate" as the CO2 escapes. When the surface starts to flatten, the bloom is over and you can continue.

Fresher coffee equals more dramatic bloom. If your coffee doesn't expand during the bloom, it probably has more than 3-4 weeks post-roast and has lost most of its CO2 and freshness.

## Agitation: moving to extract

Agitation is any movement that accelerates extraction by breaking the diffusion layer around each coffee ground. Imagine each ground particle surrounded by a bubble of saturated water — without movement, that layer stays there and extraction slows. Agitation constantly renews the water in contact with the coffee.

Ways to agitate in pour-over:

**Pouring height:** Pouring from higher up increases turbulence on impact. More agitation, more extraction. Pouring closer to the bed is gentler.

**Jet speed:** A fine, delicate stream agitates less than a thick, fast one.

**Circular pattern vs. center:** Pouring in circles moves the coffee bed more. Pouring only at center is more passive but can create channels.

**Swirl (spinning the dripper):** After pouring, you can give a gentle swirl to the dripper to level the coffee bed and distribute water. James Hoffmann popularized swirling in his V60 technique — he calls it "Rao spin" in honor of Scott Rao.

**Stirring with a spoon:** Some techniques (like Tetsu Kasuya's) include stirring the bloom with an agitator. Maximum agitation, maximum extraction during that phase.

The key is consistency. If you agitate differently each time, your extraction will be different each time. Choose a technique and repeat it with precision.

## Laminar vs. turbulent flow

When water passes through the coffee bed, it can do so in two ways:

**Laminar flow:** Water moves in parallel, orderly layers without chaotic mixing. Extraction is predictable and uniform. This is what you want.

**Turbulent flow:** Water moves chaotically with swirls and irregular mixing. Extraction is unpredictable — some zones over-extract, others under-extract. This is channeling in pour-over.

Factors that promote laminar flow: uniform grind (good burr grinder), homogeneous coffee distribution in the filter, constant and controlled pouring, level coffee bed.

Factors that cause turbulence: coffee clumps, erratic or aggressive pouring, fines (ultra-small particles) that migrate to the bottom and block flow, bumps to the dripper during extraction.

## Bypass: the water that doesn't extract

In a pour-over, not all water passes through the coffee. A portion flows along the filter walls without touching the coffee bed — this is called **bypass**. Bypass dilutes your beverage without extracting additional compounds.

Is it bad? Not necessarily. A little bypass can soften a coffee that would otherwise be too intense. But too much bypass produces a thin, under-extracted cup.

Different drippers have different bypass levels:

- **V60:** Low bypass — the filter makes nearly total contact with the coffee thanks to the conical design and spiral ridges. High, direct extraction.
- **Chemex:** Moderate bypass — the thick filter and air channel ridges create some space.
- **Kalita Wave:** More bypass — the flat bottom with three small holes creates an air chamber between the filter and the wavy walls. The coffee extracts more evenly but with more dilution.
- **Clever Dripper:** Zero bypass during immersion (water doesn't come out until you place it on the cup), then normal filtration.

## The variables of pour-over

Let's review the variables from Lesson 1.3 applied specifically to pour-over:

**Grind:** Medium to medium-coarse, like coarse table salt. Finer than for French press, coarser than for espresso. The exact range depends on the dripper and filter.

**Ratio:** The SCA standard is 1:16.67 (60g of coffee per liter of water). In practice, between 1:15 (more concentrated) and 1:17 (lighter) works. A good starting point: **15g of coffee, 250g of water** (ratio 1:16.7).

**Temperature:** 92-96°C. Generally higher than for espresso because without pressure you need more thermal energy to extract.

**Total time:** 2:30 to 4:00 minutes depending on the method and dose. If your drawdown (time from your last pour until the last drop falls) is much faster or slower, adjust the grind.

**Pouring structure:** How many pouring phases, how much water in each phase, how long you wait between phases. This is what differentiates one technique from another.

## The importance of the grinder

In espresso, a bad grinder produces channeling. In pour-over, a bad grinder produces fines — ultra-small particles that migrate to the bottom of the filter and clog it, causing an endless drawdown. Your coffee over-extracts, tastes bitter, and takes 6 minutes instead of 3.

A good burr grinder produces more uniformly-sized particles with fewer fines. For pour-over, conical burr grinders produce slightly more fines than flat burr grinders, but both work well if they're quality.

Recommended grinders for pour-over:
- **Entry-level:** Timemore C2/C3, 1Zpresso Q2 (manual, $50-80 USD)
- **Mid-range:** Comandante C40, 1Zpresso JX-Pro (manual, $150-250 USD)
- **High-end:** Baratza Encore ESP, Fellow Ode Gen 2 (electric, $150-300 USD)

## What you need for this lesson

- A pour-over dripper (V60, Chemex, Kalita Wave — any of them)
- Corresponding filters
- A gooseneck kettle — this is important. A regular kettle won't give you the flow control you need
- A scale with a timer
- Fresh coffee (7-21 days post-roast)

## Practical exercise

Prepare two pour-overs of the same coffee changing only the agitation:

1. **Shot A:** Gentle bloom (no stirring), quiet pouring from low height, no swirl
2. **Shot B:** Aggressive bloom (stir with a spoon), pouring from higher up, swirl at the end

Keep everything else the same: dose, ratio, grind, temperature, pouring pattern. Taste them side by side. Shot B should have more extraction — more sweetness but potentially more bitterness. Which do you prefer?

## Key concepts from this lesson

- The bloom releases CO2 and prepares the coffee for even extraction — always do it
- Agitation (pouring, swirl, stirring) accelerates extraction; be consistent
- Laminar flow = even extraction; turbulence = channeling
- Bypass varies by dripper: V60 (little) vs Kalita (more) vs Clever (zero in immersion)
- Standard pour-over ratio: 1:15 to 1:17, temperature 92-96°C, time 2:30-4:00
- A gooseneck kettle is essential for flow control
- Grinder fines clog filters — invest in a good grinder
