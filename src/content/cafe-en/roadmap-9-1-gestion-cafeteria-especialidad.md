## From barista to entrepreneur: understanding the business side

Many talented baristas dream of owning their own café. But there's a chasm between knowing how to pull a good espresso and understanding how to operate a profitable business. Some of the best baristas have failed as owners because they didn't understand the numbers. This chapter bridges that gap.

A specialty coffee shop isn't a luxury — it's a business with tight margins that requires obsessive operational discipline. Every drink you serve has a specific cost. Every minute of the shift counts. Every decision about suppliers directly impacts your profitability.

## The menu: simplicity is profitability

A classic mistake: new owners create enormous menus. 40 different drinks. 20 food options. The result is chaos at the bar, customer confusion, and uncontrollable costs.

A successful café builds its menu around **three espresso drinks and three non-espresso alternatives**:

**Core espresso drinks:**
- Espresso (1-2oz)
- Cappuccino (classic 1:2 espresso-microfoam)
- Flat white (1:3 espresso-velluto)

**Non-espresso:**
- Filter coffee (Chemex, V60, or Kalita Wave)
- Cold brew seasonal
- Rotating specialty drink (seasonal, experimental)

Why does this work? Each drink you add multiplies your operational complexity. It requires:
- Additional staff training
- Extra inventory
- Waste risk
- Customer decision time (which slows the bar)

Menu design at successful specialty cafés (Bluestone Lane, Stumptown, Square Mile) follows this principle: **do few things, do them perfectly, build consistency around that**.

## Pricing: the margin equation

Here comes the part that scares many baristas: business math.

**Typical cost structure per espresso drink:**
- Coffee bean: $0.50-$0.75 (10-12g)
- Milk (if applicable): $0.30-$0.50
- Cups, lids, sleeves: $0.15-$0.25
- Gas/electricity/water: $0.20-$0.30
- **Total cost: $1.15-$1.80 per drink**

If you sell a cappuccino for $5:
- Direct cost: $1.50
- Gross margin: $3.50 (70%)

Sounds good? It's not enough. That 70% must cover:
- Rent
- Staff salaries
- Equipment maintenance
- Insurance and permits
- Marketing
- Net profit

A healthy F&B margin is 65-70% cost of goods. This means **final price = direct cost ÷ 0.35 or 0.30**.

Example: if your coffee with milk costs $1.50 in ingredients:
- At 35% cost of goods → Price: $4.30
- At 30% cost of goods → Price: $5.00

Many specialty cafés in major cities price drinks at $5-$6. This isn't greed — it's viability math.

## Operating costs: where margins disappear

Here's where most owners don't see the money:

**Rent:** Generally 20-30% of gross monthly revenue. A café with $12,000 USD in monthly revenue should ideally pay no more than $2,400-$3,600 in rent.

**Labor cost:** 25-35% of revenue. A full-time barista earns $2,000-$3,500 monthly. A café manager earns $3,000-$5,000. An owner working full-time needs competitive salary ($4,000+).

**Supplies:** Napkins, stirrers, sugar, oat milk, pastries. Generally 3-5% of revenue.

**Utilities:** Water, gas, electricity. 3-5% for a quick-service café.

**Technical maintenance:** Espresso machines require service. Grinders need recalibration. Budget 2-3% of annual revenue.

**Shrinkage:** Coffee prepared and discarded, milk spillage, mistakes. Expect 3-5% of your goods costs.

## Relationship with suppliers and roasters

A specialty café is only as good as its suppliers. This relationship determines:
- Coffee quality at your bar
- Price you pay
- Product consistency
- Access to special lots

**Negotiating with roasters:**
- Small volumes cost more. 10kg/month from a roaster might be $8-$10/kg. 50kg/month drops to $6-$7/kg.
- Negotiate payment terms: 30 days? Advance?
- Develop a relationship — ask about processes, visit the roastery if you can, participate in cups.
- Don't change suppliers monthly. Consistency is valuable.

**Milk suppliers:**
- Negotiate with 2-3 local distributors for backup
- Price varies enormously — good milk for microfoam is $0.40-$0.60 per liter at volume
- Try different brands — which one texturizes best?

**Equipment and maintenance:**
- Establish a trusted technician who knows your machine
- "Preventive" service costs less than emergencies
- Budget for chemical descaling every 3 months

## KPIs: how to know if you're making money

The numbers that matter:

**Average ticket:** How much does a customer spend on average? In specialty cafés it's $5-$8 USD. Below $5, reevaluate pricing. Above $8, you're probably selling pastries + drinks (good sign).

**Transactions per hour:** During peak hours, a well-organized bar with 1-2 baristas does 15-25 transactions/hour. If it's 8-10, there's operational inefficiency. If it's 30+, it's chaos and quality suffers.

**Cash rotation:** If your coffee inventory costs $5,000, how long before that converts to money? Ideally less than 3 weeks. If your coffee expires before selling it, you bought too much.

**Operating margin:** Revenue minus ALL costs (including your salary). A healthy café has 10-15% operating margin. Below 10% and you're in dangerous territory.

**Labor cost per transaction:** If your staff costs $3,000/month and you do 3,000 transactions, it's $1 per transaction. At $6 average ticket, that's 16%. Healthy.

## Space design and bar flow

Your café's architecture directly affects your serving capacity:

**Espresso bar:** Ideally 3-4 meters long. Espresso machine in the center. Grinders on each side. Pitcher station above the machine. This setup lets 2 baristas work without crashing.

**Customer space:** Enough for people to queue without blocking the entrance. A 5-minute wait is normal. A 15+ minute wait means you need another bar or extra staff.

**Grinder:** Critical position. Should be near the customer but not directly at the bar (noise distracts). Low-burr grinders are unsustainable in professional café — invest in a good conical grinder (Baratza Sette, Fellow Ode) or better yet, a commercial grinder (Mythos, Nino).

**Wash station and storage:** Away from customer view. Clean pitchers magically disappear if there's no system.

## Break-even point: when you start making money

Here's the question every new owner asks: when do I hit break-even?

**Simple calculation:**
- Fixed monthly costs (rent, utilities, minimum salary): $6,000
- Gross margin per drink: 70% of price
- Average price: $6
- Margin per transaction: $4.20

Break-even: $6,000 ÷ $4.20 = 1,428 transactions/month = 48 transactions/day (assuming 30 open days).

In a café in a good location, this typically happens in 3-6 months. In difficult locations, 12+ months. Most cafés lose money the first 6 months.

## What you need for this lesson

A notebook and a calculator. Better yet, an Excel sheet where you calculate:
- Current price of your drinks
- Cost of ingredients for each
- Percentage margin
- Average daily transactions

## Practical exercise

If you work in a café, ask the owner to see the numbers (or if you're the owner, do this now):
1. What's the coffee cost per drink?
2. What's the average ticket?
3. How many transactions per day?
4. What's the estimated gross margin?

Then calculate the operating margin. Subtract from total monthly revenue: cost of goods, rent, utilities, salaries, shrinkage. What's left? That's your real operating margin.

## Key concepts from this lesson

- A small menu (6 drinks) is more profitable and consistent than a large one
- The equation is simple: direct cost shouldn't exceed 30-35% of final price
- Negotiate with suppliers, build relationships, don't change monthly
- Real costs are hidden: shrinkage, service, maintenance
- Monitor transactions/hour, average ticket, and operating margin weekly
- Break-even typically happens 3-6 months after opening
- Being a good barista and good businessperson are different skills — work on both
