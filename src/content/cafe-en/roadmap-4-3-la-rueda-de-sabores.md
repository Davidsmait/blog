## A map for what you feel

The first time someone describes coffee as "citric with notes of jasmine and a brown sugar finish," it can sound pretentious. Does it really taste like jasmine? Isn't it just... coffee?

The short answer: yes, it can really taste like jasmine. The chemical compounds that produce jasmine aroma (like linalool) are present in both jasmine flowers and certain coffees. It's not metaphor — it's chemistry.

The long answer: you need vocabulary to perceive. This sounds counter-intuitive, but there's evidence that naming what you perceive improves your ability to perceive it. If you only have the words "good" and "bad" for coffee, your brain settles for that resolution. If you have a map with dozens of specific descriptors, your brain starts looking for and finding those nuances.

The Coffee Taster's Flavor Wheel from the SCA is that map. It was originally published in 1995 and completely revised in 2016 in collaboration with World Coffee Research, and it's probably the most important document in specialty coffee.

## How to read the wheel

The wheel is read from center outward, from general to specific:

**First ring (center):** Broad categories. There are 9 main families: fruity, sour/fermented, green/vegetable, other, roasted, spices, nut/cocoa, sweet, and floral.

**Second ring:** Subcategories. For example, within "fruity" you find: citric, berries, dried fruit, stone fruit, and other fruits.

**Third ring (outer):** Specific descriptors. Within "citric" you find: lemon, lime, orange, grapefruit. Within "berries": strawberry, blueberry, raspberry, blackberry.

The idea is that when you taste a coffee, you don't need to jump immediately to the outer ring. If you perceive something "fruity" but aren't sure what fruit, stay at that level. With practice, you'll start distinguishing whether it's fruity-citric or fruity-berries. Eventually, if it's more lemon or more orange.

## The flavor families

### Fruity
The most diverse and celebrated family in specialty coffee. African coffees (Ethiopia, Kenya, Rwanda) are particularly known for their fruity notes.

**Citric:** Lemon, orange, grapefruit, tangerine. Show up as bright, juicy acidity. Common in high-altitude washed coffees.

**Berries:** Strawberry, blueberry, raspberry, blackberry. Sweeter, rounder notes than citric. Very present in Ethiopian natural coffees (Guji, Yirgacheffe).

**Stone fruit:** Peach, plum, nectarine, cherry. Velvety sweetness with moderate acidity. Frequent in Colombian and Central American coffees.

**Dried fruit:** Raisins, dates, figs. Concentrated sweetness, almost caramelized. Appears in natural Brazilian coffees and honey-processed coffees.

**Tropical fruit:** Mango, pineapple, passion fruit, guava. Exuberant sweetness with vibrant acidity. Typical of anaerobically processed coffees or exceptional naturals.

### Sweet
Not just the presence of sweetness, but the type of sweetness.

**Caramel/brown sugar:** Cooked sugar, honey, molasses, maple syrup. It's "brown" sweetness — warm, comforting. Very common in Latin American medium-roasted coffees.

**Vanilla:** Delicate sweetness with aromatic depth. Can appear in barrel-aged coffees or specially processed ones.

**Chocolate:** Cocoa, milk chocolate, dark chocolate. Probably the most universal descriptor in coffee. Present in Brazil, Colombia, Guatemala, Mexico. It's the comfort-note par excellence.

### Nut/Cocoa
Roasted, warm, comforting flavors.

**Nuts:** Almond, hazelnut, pecan, peanut. Softness and roundness. Very present in Brazilian and Mexican coffees.

**Cocoa:** The flavor of cocoa before it's sweetened. Has a slight pleasant bitterness. Frequent in medium to dark roasts.

### Floral
The most difficult descriptors for beginners to perceive, but unmistakable when you find them.

**Jasmine:** Sweet, fragrant, almost perfumed. The classic descriptor of washed Ethiopian Yirgacheffe.

**Rose:** More delicate than jasmine. Appears in Panamanian Geshas and some East African coffees.

**Orange blossom:** Citric-floral. Can appear in coffees with complex citric notes.

**Tea (yes, like tea):** Some coffees have a character that reminds you of black tea or Earl Grey. This is particularly common in washed Ethiopian coffees and Geshas. It's a combination of floral delicacy, light body, and subtle tannins.

### Spices
Warm or peppery notes that add complexity.

**Cinnamon:** Sweet and warm. Frequent in Mexican and Guatemalan coffees.

**Clove:** More sharp than cinnamon. Can appear in Sumatran or Sulawesi coffees.

**Pepper:** A subtle spicy note that appears in some African coffees or roasts that've developed Maillard reactions extensively.

### Roasted
Notes produced by the roasting process (Maillard reactions and caramelization).

**Cereal/malt:** Toast, cookies, cereal. Common in well-executed medium roasts.

**Tobacco:** Dry, earthy complexity. Not necessarily negative — can be an interesting nuance in Sumatran or Indian coffees.

**Smoky:** Can be intentional (controlled roast) or defect (uncontrolled roast). In small amounts it adds depth; in excess, it masks everything.

### Green/Vegetable
Generally undesirable in roasted coffee, though with nuances.

**Herbaceous:** Hay, grass, green tea. In small amounts can be an interesting nuance. In excess, it indicates under-roasting.

**Vegetable:** Peas, potato, cucumber. Almost always a defect — indicates immature beans (quakers), very light roast, or old coffee.

### Sour/Fermented
The line between "interesting controlled fermentation" and "defect" is thin.

**Vinous:** Like red wine. Can be desirable in naturals with controlled fermentation. Adds complexity and depth.

**Acetic/vinegary:** Like vinegar. Almost always a defect from excessive fermentation. If you perceive this, the coffee probably has a processing problem.

**Fermented:** Smell and taste of overripe or rotten fruit. Clear defect.

## The spaces between descriptors

One thing the wheel doesn't show are the spaces between segments. In the 2016 version, these spaces are deliberate: if two descriptors are separated by a large gap, their chemical profiles are very different. If they're close together with no gap, they're more similar.

For example, "chocolate" and "dark chocolate" are next to each other — they're close perceptions. But "chocolate" and "cherry" are separated by more space — they're distinct perceptions, though they can coexist in the same coffee.

## You don't need the wheel to start

A common beginner mistake is looking at the wheel during tasting and trying to "find" flavors in it. Don't do that. The wheel is a reference for afterward, not a checklist.

The correct process is: taste the coffee, note what you perceive in your own words ("something sweet, like cooked fruit"), then consult the wheel to find a descriptor that matches what you felt ("dried fruit — raisins"). Over time, the vocabulary integrates and you don't need to consult the wheel.

Your personal perception is always valid. If a coffee reminds you of your grandmother's cookies, that's a legitimate descriptor. The SCA wheel is a communication tool — it helps you talk with others about what you perceive. It's not absolute truth about what flavors a coffee "should" have.

## Negative descriptors and defects

The wheel also includes negative descriptors that indicate problems:

**Phenolic/medicinal:** Taste like a Band-Aid, iodine, medicine. Serious defect, usually from contamination during processing.

**Rubber:** Burned, like a tire. Indicates damage during roasting (scorched beans) or contamination.

**Potato (potato defect):** Smell and taste like raw potato. Exclusive to the African Great Lakes region (Rwanda, Burundi). Caused by a bacterium (Pantoea). It's an individual bean defect — a single affected bean can ruin the whole cup.

**Moldy:** Humidity, basement, wet cardboard. Indicates improper storage or fungal contamination.

**Earthy:** Taste like dirt or dust. Can come from contamination during drying on the ground, or from old beans (past-crop coffee).

If you detect any of these, it's not your palate or your technique — it's the coffee. And that's also valuable information.

## Practical exercise

Do a comparative tasting of 2-3 coffees and use the wheel as reference:

1. Prepare the bowls (simplified SCA method from previous lesson)
2. For each coffee, without looking at the wheel, write 3-5 words describing what you perceive. Use your own words, unfiltered.
3. Now consult the wheel. Do your descriptors match any family? Can you be more specific? If you wrote "something sweet," is it sweet-chocolate, sweet-caramel, or sweet-fruity?
4. Repeat at different temperatures. Descriptors change as the coffee cools.
5. Compare your notes with the coffee bag's tasting notes (if it has them). Don't worry if they don't match — what matters is you're developing your own vocabulary.

Over time, this exercise trains your brain to look for and name nuances you previously overlooked.

## Key concepts from this lesson

- The SCA Flavor Wheel is a map of descriptors organized from center (general) to outer (specific)
- Naming what you perceive improves your ability to perceive it — vocabulary trains your palate
- The 9 main families are: fruity, sour/fermented, green/vegetable, other, roasted, spices, nut/cocoa, sweet, and floral
- Don't look for flavors in the wheel; describe first with your own words then consult the wheel
- Negative descriptors (phenolic, rubber, moldy) are information about coffee defects, not your palate
- Your personal perception is always valid; the wheel is a communication tool, not absolute authority
- Coffees from different origins and processing tend to fall into different flavor families
