## Shape matters more than you think

In Phase 3 you learned to use different drippers: V60, Chemex, Kalita Wave, Clever. You knew they produced different flavors. But you probably attributed those differences to the filter or the dripper's "style." The reality is deeper: dripper geometry — its shape, angle, ribs, and drainage hole — determines how water flows through the coffee, and therefore how it extracts.

Gagné analyzes drippers as fluid engineering systems. Each design decision has measurable consequences on flow, extraction uniformity, and flavor profile. This lesson gives you tools to understand why each dripper tastes the way it does.

## Conical vs flat

### Conical drippers (V60, Chemex)

A conical dripper directs water toward a central drainage point. This has several physical consequences.

**Flow concentration:** Water converges toward the bottom center. Particles in the bed center receive more flow than those at the edges. This creates an extraction gradient: the center extracts more than the edges.

**Variable coffee bed:** The bed is thicker in the center (where cone walls converge) and thinner at the edges. According to Darcy's law, water takes the path of least resistance — the thinner edges. Gagné documents that in a V60, up to 30% of water can "sneak" through the edges without passing through the center of the bed.

**Drainage speed:** The V60's single large hole allows fast drainage. This gives the barista more control: you can manipulate time by adjusting grind and technique. But it also means technique errors have more impact.

### Flat-bottomed drippers (Kalita Wave, April, Fellow Stagg XF)

A flat bottom distributes drainage over a wider area through multiple small holes.

**Uniform distribution:** Water drains through multiple points, not one. This reduces the center-vs-edge extraction gradient. The coffee bed has more uniform thickness.

**Self-regulation:** The multiple small holes act as flow "restriction." This makes drainage speed less dependent on grind and technique, and more dependent on dripper design. Result: more consistency between brews, less dependence on barista skill.

**Lower dynamic range:** The downside is you have less control over time. The dripper "regulates" flow for you. For baristas wanting to manipulate every variable, this feels limiting. For coffee shops needing consistency across shifts, it's an advantage.

### The Chemex case

The Chemex is technically conical, but its thick paper filter changes the equation. Chemex filters are ~2x thicker than standard V60 filters. This acts as additional flow restriction and more aggressive filtration of oils and fines. The result is notably cleaner, lighter coffee than a V60, despite sharing conical geometry, because the filter dominates over shape.

## The cone angle

### V60: 60 degrees

The V60 gets its name from its angle: 60 degrees. This angle creates a balance between bed depth (deeper = more contact time) and drainage speed (steeper = faster).

### Narrower angles

A tighter cone (smaller angle) creates a deeper, narrower bed. Water travels more distance through coffee, increasing contact time. But the narrow bed means water has less surface area to penetrate — it can create more channeling because flow concentrates in a smaller space.

### Wider angles

A wider cone (larger angle, approaching flat) distributes coffee in a thinner, wider layer. Water travels less distance but with more contact area. Less potential channeling, but less contact time per unit water.

Gagné argues there's no "correct" angle — each angle is a trade-off between contact time and flow uniformity. The V60 at 60° is an elegant compromise, but not optimal for all coffees or brewing styles.

## Ribs and air channels

### What are ribs

Ribs are grooves in the interior walls of a dripper. The V60 has spiral ribs running from top to bottom. Other drippers have shorter, straight ribs, or none at all.

### Their function

Ribs serve two critical functions.

**Separate filter from wall.** Without ribs, wet filter adheres to the dripper wall, sealing the edges. This forces all water to drain only through the bottom, slowing flow and creating dead zones where coffee doesn't extract.

**Create air channels.** The space between ribs lets air escape through the edges while water drains below. Without these air channels, a partial vacuum forms that slows drainage — like trying to empty an inverted bottle where air can't enter.

### V60 ribs

The V60's spiral ribs extend all the way up the cone. This lets air escape freely at all times, even when water level is high. The result is fast, consistent drainage. If ribs were shorter (as on some cheap clones), drainage would be slower when water level is high, because the filter seals against the wall above the ribs.

### Drippers without ribs

Some flat-bottomed drippers (like the Kalita Wave) use no ribs but instead rely on the wavy filter to create separation. The wavy filter maintains distance between itself and dripper walls, creating air spaces. It's an elegant solution that shifts rib function to the disposable filter.

## Filters: the invisible component

### Paper vs metal vs cloth

**Paper:** Retains oils (diterpenes like cafestol and kahweol) and fines. Produces clean, bright coffee with defined acidity. Paper filters are the pour-over standard because oil and fines retention contributes to flavor clarity.

**Metal (fine mesh):** Lets oils and some fines pass through. Produces coffee with more body, more texture, more "weight" in the mouth. Similar to French press character but in pour-over format.

**Cloth:** A middle ground. Retains fines but lets more oils pass than paper. Produces medium body with good clarity. Requires maintenance (cleaning, wet storage) that makes it impractical for most.

### Paper thickness

Not all paper filters are equal. Gagné measures that filter thickness significantly affects drain time. Chemex filters are the thickest and retain the most. Standard white V60 filters are intermediate. Japanese filters (like Cafec) vary by model.

A thicker filter acts as additional resistance in Darcy's law: it reduces flow regardless of grind. This is relevant when you switch filter brands and your recipe "stops working" — you may need to adjust grind to compensate.

### Bleached vs natural

Bleached filters are processed with oxygen (not chlorine in modern brands). Natural (brown) filters aren't. The flavor difference is detectable: natural filters can impart a papery taste if not rinsed well. Gagné recommends rinsing with hot water regardless of type to eliminate residue and preheat.

### Pre-wetting the filter

Rinsing the filter with hot water before brewing is standard, but Gagné explains *why* it matters beyond flavor: wet paper changes its flow properties. Dry paper has loose fibers that can migrate into your coffee. Wet paper settles against the dripper and creates a more uniform seal. Flow through wet paper is more predictable than through dry paper.

## The bypass effect

### What is it

In conical drippers, part of the water can flow down the walls between filter and ribs without passing through the coffee bed. This is called "bypass" — water that skips the coffee and goes straight to the cup.

### How much bypass

Gagné estimates that in a V60, bypass can represent 5-15% of total water, depending on pouring technique, grind size (coarser = lower bed = more wall exposed), and water level (high water = more lateral pressure = more bypass).

### Is it good or bad?

It depends. Bypass dilutes the coffee, reducing TDS and concentration. But it also reduces over-extraction because part of the water doesn't extract anything. Some baristas use bypass intentionally: prepare concentrated coffee (less water through the bed, finer grind) then dilute with hot water. This is essentially the "americano" technique applied to pour-over, letting you achieve desired concentration and extraction independently.

Jonathan Gagné argues that understanding bypass is essential for reproducible recipes. If your dripper has more bypass than someone else's (due to rib differences, filter, or technique), the same recipe produces different results even though the "numbers" are identical.

## Practical exercise

### Conical vs flat

1. Prepare the same coffee with V60 and Kalita Wave (or any flat dripper): same ratio (15:250), same temperature, same grind
2. Record: total drain time, final bed appearance
3. Compare flavor: which is cleaner? Which has more body?
4. Adjust the flat-bottom dripper's grind to match V60 drain time. Does the flavor comparison change?

### Effect of ribs

1. Prepare one cup in your V60 normally
2. For the second, wrap the V60's exterior with tape (covering the ribs) to simulate a rib-less dripper, forcing the filter against the wall
3. Compare: how does drain time change? Is there flavor difference?

### Filter comparison

1. If you have access to filters of different brands or types (bleached vs natural, thick vs thin), prepare the same coffee with each
2. Keep everything else constant
3. Document differences in drain time and flavor

## Key concepts from this lesson

- Dripper shape (conical vs flat) determines how water distributes extraction: conical concentrates flow in center, flat distributes uniformly
- Ribs separate filter from wall and create essential air channels for consistent drainage
- Filter is an extraction variable: its thickness, material, and treatment affect flow and flavor as much as grind
- Bypass (water that skips the coffee bed) is a significant factor in conical drippers affecting final concentration
- No dripper is "better" — each design is a set of trade-offs between control, consistency, uniformity, and flavor character
- Changing filter or dripper without adjusting recipe is as problematic as changing coffee without adjusting
- Understanding geometry lets you diagnose problems that were previously mysterious
